# Pitch Deck Rubric

## Definition

A scoring rubric for evaluating the quality of a B2B SaaS pitch deck across six dimensions. This rubric produces a diagnostic that identifies exactly where a deck is strong, where it breaks, and what to fix first.

## What This Rubric Is For

This rubric evaluates **pitch decks** -- the narrative delivery format that compresses the POV into slides and delivers it live or async. A pitch deck has one job: to move the audience from "I have this problem" to "this company is the answer" in roughly 10 content slides.

A pitch deck is not a messaging framework. It is not a POV narrative. It is not a homepage. It is graded on slide-level clarity, narrative sequence, and emotional impact -- not structural completeness (messaging framework criteria), long-form writing quality (POV criteria), or page architecture (homepage criteria). Each document type has different evaluation criteria because each serves a different function:

- A **pitch deck** is a narrative delivery format. It must carry the full argument across a sequence of slides, each making exactly one point, with headlines that tell the story alone. It is graded on headline narrative, arc integrity, focus and density, problem-solution mirroring, proof quality, and standout factor.
- A **messaging framework** is a structured reference architecture. It is graded on structural completeness, pillar construction, buyer specificity, and competitive clarity -- criteria that are irrelevant for a deck.
- A **POV narrative** is a persuasive story. It is graded on opening hook, arc integrity, and emotional momentum -- criteria that overlap with but are distinct from deck evaluation.
- A **homepage** is a compressed expression of the framework. It is graded on above-the-fold clarity, page architecture, and CTA design.

The reference standard: a strong initial pitch deck is roughly 10 content slides. The corpus gold standard is a home services AI platform (Series B) whose headline sequence alone tells the full story -- moving from the daily pain of revenue leakage, through the structural failure of the buyer's default staffing plan, to the growth ceiling consequence, and finally introducing the AI front office as the inevitable resolution, closing with a division of labor that leaves the operator focused on their craft.

## Positioning Stance -- Read This First

Every review must account for which positioning stance the asset is pursuing. The stance dictates what "good" looks like -- the same dimension can demand opposite execution depending on the stance. Before evaluating any dimension, determine which stance applies.

### Stance 1: Creating a New Category

- **Reference point:** a job-to-be-done of your choosing
- **Competitive alternative:** the status quo way people do that JTBD (NOT named vendors)
- **Target customer:** people doing the JTBD you selected, who don't know a category exists
- **What good looks like:** indicts the status quo approach/architecture, not competitors. Names a new category. Educates the buyer that the problem is real and unsolved. Invokes an inevitable future. Longest game, highest risk, highest reward.
- **Stance error to flag:** naming or comparing against competitor brands. A category creator who benchmarks against named vendors is undermining their own category -- they're implicitly admitting they're in someone else's category.

### Stance 2: Competing in a Mature Category

- **Reference point:** the category name itself (the category already exists in buyers' minds)
- **Competitive alternative:** other named vendors in the category
- **Target customer:** people already shopping the category
- **What good looks like:** sharp, specific differentiation against named competitors. Clear reason-to-choose-us within an understood frame. Mind space and budget already exist -- the job is to win the comparison, not create the need.
- **Stance error to flag:** failing to differentiate against named competitors, or trying to educate the buyer on a problem they already understand. In a mature category, spending the asset explaining why the problem matters is wasted -- the buyer knows. Vague "we're better" claims without specific head-to-head contrast also fail.

### Stance 3: Competing in an Immature Category

- **Reference point:** the job-to-be-done of the category
- **Competitive alternative:** the status quo way people do that JTBD
- **Target customer:** people doing the JTBD who may not know the category exists yet
- **What good looks like:** balances light category education with differentiation. Positions to become a leader as the category solidifies. Some teaching required, but not from scratch. Indicts the status quo while gesturing at the emerging category.
- **Stance error to flag:** positioning as if the category is mature (over-indexing on competitor comparison when buyers don't yet know the category) OR positioning as if creating from scratch (over-educating when an emerging category already has some awareness). The immature stance requires calibration between the two.

## How Stance Changes This Evaluation

The positioning stance shifts what each dimension should look for:

- **Headline Narrative Test:** For a category creator, the headline sequence must build a category argument -- the headlines should take the audience from "here's how you do this JTBD today" through "here's why that approach is broken" to "here's the new category that solves it." For a mature-category player, the headline sequence should build a comparison argument -- "here's the problem you're already shopping for" through "here's why we win." For an immature-category player, the headlines must balance education and differentiation.

- **Arc Integrity:** A category creator's deck needs more Context and Problem stage real estate to build the category -- the audience must be educated on why the category needs to exist before the solution can land. Expect 3-4 slides before the reveal. A mature-category deck can move faster through Context/Problem (the buyer already knows the category) and allocate more slides to differentiation and proof. An immature-category deck needs moderate Problem stage investment -- enough to frame the emerging category, not so much that it feels like a lecture.

- **Focus & Density:** Stance does not change this dimension's bar. Every slide makes one point regardless of stance.

- **Solution Mirrors Problem:** For a category creator, the solution slides must resolve the status quo pains, not competitive pains -- the mirror is "old JTBD approach" vs. "new category approach." For a mature-category player, the solution slides should explicitly contrast with how named competitors solve each pain. For an immature-category player, the solution mirrors the status quo failure while also gesturing at why this approach is superior to early category entrants.

- **Proof Quality & Placement:** For a category creator, proof must demonstrate that the new category works -- early adopter wins and outcome metrics that validate the category's viability. Named-vendor comparison metrics are a stance error. For a mature-category player, proof should include head-to-head wins, migration stories, and competitive benchmarks. For an immature-category player, proof should demonstrate both category viability and company leadership.

- **Standout Factor:** For a category creator, the emotional anchor should be the status quo's structural failure -- dramatize why the current JTBD approach guarantees bad outcomes. For a mature-category player, the anchor should be the moment the buyer realizes the competitor they were considering can't actually deliver. For an immature-category player, the anchor should make the buyer feel the emerging category is inevitable and this company is leading it.

## Stance-Execution Mismatch

Before scoring, determine whether the asset's actual execution matches the stance the user selected. A common and costly error is choosing one stance strategically but executing as if pursuing another. Examples:

- Selected "creating a category" but the asset is full of head-to-head competitor comparisons (executing as mature-category)
- Selected "mature category" but the asset spends most of its energy educating the buyer on a problem they already understand (executing as category creation)
- Selected "immature category" but positions with no category education at all (executing as mature) or pure greenfield education (executing as creation)

When you detect a mismatch, this is one of the most valuable findings you can surface. Name it explicitly. Explain what stance the execution actually reflects versus what they selected. Then guide them to commit to their chosen direction -- help them stay the course. The goal is not to tell them which stance is right; it's to help them execute consistently with the strategic direction they've chosen. A confused stance produces confused positioning.

## The Slide Taxonomy

Every slide in a pitch deck serves a specific function within the narrative arc. This is the genomic sequence model -- the taxonomy of slide types a deck draws from, organized by stage:

### Context Stage
- **Company credibility intro** -- who we are and why we're credible (used sparingly; avoid opening with this)
- **Team** -- founders, advisors, domain expertise
- **Agenda / meeting setup** -- what we'll cover today (optional, often verbal)
- **Audience-centric discovery** -- "what we learned about your world" (powerful when drawn from real research)
- **Strategic context / market shift** -- what changed in the world that makes this urgent now
- **Unique insight / POV** -- the company's proprietary point of view on the market

### Problem Stage
- **Core problem** -- "why buy anything" -- the hero problem slide
- **Emotional anchor / enemy** -- the "bogeyman" slide that dramatizes the villain (optional but actively rewarded)
- **Stakes / negative consequences** -- what happens if the problem goes unsolved
- **Urgency trigger** -- "why act now" -- temporal pressure, regulatory change, competitive threat
- **Failed alternatives** -- what the buyer has already tried and why it can't work

### Solution Stage
- **Platform reveal** -- the "hinge" slide where the product enters as the inevitable answer
- **Differentiation** -- "why buy us" -- what makes this approach structurally different
- **Capabilities proof** -- how the product actually works (mechanism, not feature list)
- **Solution overview** -- the full platform at a glance
- **Applied benefits / use cases** -- the product applied to the buyer's specific workflows
- **Platform validation / architecture** -- technical credibility for technical buyers
- **Adoption path / maturity model** -- how implementation works and what the ramp looks like

### Proof Stage
- **Business case / ROI** -- quantified financial impact with named mechanisms
- **Vision fit / future state** -- what the buyer's world looks like after adoption
- **Social proof / customer logos** -- peer validation from recognizable companies
- **Analyst validation** -- third-party credibility (Gartner, Forrester, etc.)
- **Case proof / customer story** -- a named customer with a specific, traceable outcome

### Close Stage
- **Call to action / next steps** -- specific ask matched to the conversation stage

Not every deck needs every slide type. A 10-slide deck selects the right subset for the audience and stage. But the stages must appear in order: **Context -> Problem -> Solution -> Proof -> Close.** A deck that opens with Solution or buries Problem after Proof has a structural failure, not a style preference.

## The Six Dimensions

### 1. Headline Narrative Test

**What to evaluate:**

- **Read only the slide headlines in order. Do they tell the complete argument without any body content?** This is the single most important test. A partner should be able to forward the deck to a colleague who reads only headlines and still understand why this company matters.
- **Is each headline a claim, not a label?** "Revenue leaks out every day" is a claim. "The Problem" is a label. "Market Overview" is a label. Labels waste the most valuable real estate on the slide -- the one line the audience always reads.
- **Does each headline advance the argument -- or repeat the previous slide's point in different words?** Repetition disguised as progression is the most common headline failure. Each headline must move the story forward.
- **Are the headlines speakable?** Every headline should work as a sentence the presenter actually says out loud. "Value Pillar 2: Customer Experience Optimization" is unspeakable. "Your front office, automated" is speakable.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Headlines alone tell the complete story in sequence. Every headline is a claim. Each advances the argument. All are speakable. |
| **4/5** | Headlines mostly tell the story but one or two are labels or restatements. |
| **3/5** | Mix of claims and labels. Reading headlines alone gives the topic but not the argument. |
| **2/5** | Mostly labels ("Problem," "Solution," "Team"). The argument lives entirely in body content. |
| **1/5** | Headlines are generic or decorative. No narrative thread. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** The corpus gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) built a headline sequence that moved from the daily pain of revenue leakage, through a structural indictment of the buyer's default staffing plan, to the growth ceiling consequence, then introduced the AI front office as the resolution, showed end-to-end coverage across the customer journey, and closed with a clean division of labor. Reading those headlines alone, you know the problem (revenue leakage), why existing approaches fail (staffing doesn't scale), the stakes (growth ceiling), the solution (AI front office), what it does (automates the front office end-to-end), and the close (the operator focuses on their craft while the platform handles the rest). Zero body content needed.
- **Weak (1-2):** The same deck retitled with labels: "The Problem" / "Staffing Challenges" / "Market Context" / "Our Solution" / "Product Overview" / "Use Cases" / "Why Choose Us." Identical content on each slide. Zero narrative. A partner who skims only headlines learns nothing except that a vendor exists.

### 2. Arc Integrity

**What to evaluate:**

- **Do the five stages appear in order: Context -> Problem -> Solution -> Proof -> Close?** This is the structural spine. Decks that open with Solution or bury Problem after Proof fail structurally.
- **Within the Problem stage: is the core pain established first, are failed alternatives indicted, and are stakes quantified BEFORE the solution reveal?** The gold standard sequence builds through three beats: daily pain, then a structural indictment of the buyer's default plan, then escalating stakes. Each beat earns the next.
- **Does the reveal slide feel earned?** This is the same test as the POV pivot. The audience should feel relief when the solution appears -- "finally, someone gets this." If the deck jumps from one problem slide straight to product, the reveal is unearned. The audience hasn't suffered enough to want the cure.
- **Does proof come AFTER the solution is established, not before?** Proof before solution is a common structural error -- it tries to build credibility before the audience knows what they're evaluating. Proof validates claims already made.
- **Is there a clear close / next step, or does the deck just end?** A deck without a close is a conversation without an ask. The close must be specific to the conversation stage -- "pilot with one team" not "let's chat."

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | All five stages in order. Problem stage builds pain -> failed alternatives -> stakes before reveal. Reveal feels earned. Clear close. |
| **4/5** | Stages in order but one Problem-stage beat is thin (e.g., failed alternatives implied, not shown). |
| **3/5** | Stages present but one is out of order, or the reveal comes after a single problem slide -- unearned. |
| **2/5** | Deck opens with product or company. Problem appears as justification after the fact. |
| **1/5** | No discernible arc. Feature tour with a logo slide. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** The gold standard deck builds the Problem stage across three slides with escalating stakes: revenue leaks (pain the buyer feels daily), then an indictment of the staffing approach (the buyer's default plan can't work), then a growth ceiling (the consequence of inaction). By the time the solution appears, the audience viscerally wants a way out. The reveal feels earned because the deck spent three slides making the audience uncomfortable with the status quo.
- **Weak (2/5):** A deck that opens with "About Us" (company history, team, funding), moves to a product overview slide, then shows a problem slide as justification: "Companies face challenges like X and Y." The problem exists to justify the product rather than the product existing to resolve a felt tension. The audience never had time to feel the pain.

### 3. Focus & Density

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does each slide make exactly ONE point?** Supporting elements are fine -- a problem slide can make one point (revenue leaks) with four supporting examples of where leaks happen. But the slide must have a single takeaway. If someone asked "what was that slide about?" there should be one answer, not two.
- **Is the deck roughly 10 content slides?** Initial pitch decks that sprawl past ~12 content slides lose focus and signal an unclear narrative. If the team couldn't choose what to cut, they haven't clarified what matters most. (Appendix or extra slides for deep-dive questions don't count against this.)
- **Can each slide be understood in under 10 seconds of looking?** The 10-second test: show the slide to someone for 10 seconds, then hide it. Can they tell you the point? If not, the slide is too dense.
- **Is there any slide that tries to be two slides -- problem AND solution, or capabilities AND proof?** Combination slides are always weaker than two focused slides. They force the audience to process two ideas simultaneously and remember neither.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Every slide makes one point, understandable in seconds. Deck is ~10 content slides. Nothing crammed. |
| **4/5** | One slide carries two ideas or the deck runs 11-12 content slides. |
| **3/5** | Two or three overloaded slides, or 13-15 content slides. |
| **2/5** | Most slides carry multiple competing points. 16+ content slides. |
| **1/5** | Wall-of-text slides throughout. No focal discipline. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** A 10-slide deck where each slide has one headline, one point, and supporting evidence that reinforces that single point. The audience never has to choose where to look or what to remember. Each slide can be absorbed in seconds.
- **Weak (2/5):** A 20-slide deck that tries to cover every product feature, every customer segment, every use case. Slides carry competing messages -- one slide is half problem framing, half product screenshot. The deck runs 45 minutes because the narrative can't focus. The audience remembers the length, not the argument.

### 4. Solution Mirrors Problem

**What to evaluate:**

- **Do the solution slides answer the problem slides directly and traceably?** The gold standard is the explicit before/after: a solution slide that lists the same revenue leaks from the problem slide and resolves each one -- "calls go unanswered" becomes "every call answered."
- **Can the buyer trace every solution claim back to a pain they just felt?** If the solution introduces capabilities that don't map to established pains, the buyer thinks "interesting, but not what I need." Every solution claim must connect to a problem the deck already made the audience feel.
- **Are there solution claims with no corresponding problem?** These are orphan capabilities -- features the deck is selling without having established why the buyer should care. Orphan capabilities signal feature-selling, not problem-solving.
- **Are there problems raised that the solution never resolves?** These are orphan pains -- tensions the deck created and then abandoned. Orphan pains undermine credibility because the audience notices the gap: "you told me this was a problem but you never showed me how you fix it."

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Every solution claim maps to an established pain. Explicit before/after structure or equivalent. No orphan capabilities or pains. |
| **4/5** | Mapping is clear but implicit -- buyer can trace it with mild effort. |
| **3/5** | Most claims map but one or two orphan capabilities appear. |
| **2/5** | Solution section is a feature tour loosely related to the problem. |
| **1/5** | No relationship between problem and solution sections. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** The gold standard deck established four specific revenue leaks in the problem section (missed calls, lost follow-ups, unworked estimates, after-hours gaps) and the solution section resolved each one explicitly: AI answers every call, automated follow-up sequences, estimate nurture campaigns, 24/7 coverage. The buyer could draw a line from each pain to its resolution. No orphans on either side.
- **Weak (2/5):** A deck spends three slides building pain around missed revenue and growth ceilings, then the solution section introduces "AI-powered analytics dashboard," "team collaboration features," and "customizable workflows." The buyer felt a revenue problem and got shown a feature tour. The solution doesn't mirror the problem -- it changes the subject.

### 5. Proof Quality & Placement

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does proof come after the solution, where it validates claims already made?** Proof before solution tries to build credibility before the audience knows what they're evaluating. Proof after solution says "here's evidence that what I just showed you actually works."
- **Are metrics specific and mechanistic?** Metrics like hundreds of thousands in after-hours revenue captured per customer and thousands of outbound jobs won from campaign activity are specific and traceable to the product's mechanism. "10x ROI" with no mechanism is marketing math the buyer can't verify.
- **Is there a case study or customer story with named outcomes -- not just a logo wall?** A logo wall says "companies use us." A named case study with a specific, traceable growth metric says something the buyer can evaluate. The second is proof. The first is decoration.
- **Is there a demo or "in action" moment that makes the product real?** At some point the audience needs to see or feel the product working. A screenshot, a live demo clip, a workflow example. Without it, the product remains abstract.
- **Are logos from the right buyer segment -- companies the prospect would recognize as peers?** A deck targeting home services owner-operators with enterprise SaaS logos has a segment mismatch. The buyer must see companies like theirs.
- **Does proof address the skeptic's question ("does this actually work?") not just the believer's ("how big can this get?")?** Vision slides serve believers. Proof slides serve skeptics. Most rooms have both. A deck with vision but no proof loses the skeptic; a deck with proof but no vision loses the champion.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Specific, mechanistic metrics. Real case study with named outcomes. Demo or in-action moment. Peer-relevant logos. Placed after solution. |
| **4/5** | Strong proof but missing one element (no demo, or case study is thin). |
| **3/5** | Proof is present but generic -- logo wall plus unverifiable metrics. |
| **2/5** | Minimal proof. One or two logos, no metrics or story. |
| **1/5** | No proof, or proof from the wrong buyer segment entirely. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** The gold standard deck featured named customers from the target segment, hard metrics tied to specific mechanisms (double-digit revenue growth, order-of-magnitude increase in bookings, significant campaign-attributed revenue from a single branch), operational proof (high booking rate, sub-three-second answer time, near-zero abandonment), all from businesses the prospect recognized as peers. Every metric traced back to a product capability established in the solution section. The skeptic in the room had nothing left to question.
- **Weak (2/5):** A logo wall of recognizable brands with no context, followed by "Trusted by 500+ companies" and a single vague quote: "Great platform, really helped our team." The buyer doesn't know what those companies achieved, whether they're in the same segment, or whether the quote describes real impact or politeness. The proof section asks the buyer to believe rather than showing them evidence.

### 6. Standout Factor

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does the deck create a "holy shit, this is the solution I've been waiting for" moment?** The audience should leave wanting to learn more -- not feeling like they sat through a vendor overview. If the deck is competent but forgettable, it fails this dimension.
- **Is there an emotional anchor -- a named enemy, a bogeyman, a dramatized villain the buyer recognizes from their own life?** Decks with an emotional anchor are actively rewarded. Indicting the buyer's own default plan -- their staffing approach that structurally can't work -- is the model anchor. It names the thing the buyer has been doing and explains why it will never work. The buyer feels caught.
- **Is there a memorable visual or framing the buyer will repeat internally?** A visual of revenue escaping the business, a growth ceiling chart -- these are moments the buyer takes into their next internal meeting. A deck without a repeatable moment is a deck that dies in the room.
- **Does the deck have a distinct voice -- or does it look and sound like every other deck in the category?** The logo-swap test applies here: could you put a competitor's logo on this deck and it would still make sense? If yes, there's no standout factor.
- **Would the champion be excited to forward this deck to their boss?** This is the ultimate test. The champion saw the pitch. Now they need to sell internally. Would they forward this deck as-is, knowing it will represent them -- or would they rewrite it first?

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Clear emotional anchor / enemy. At least one memorable visual or framing the buyer will repeat. Distinct voice. Forward-worthy. |
| **4/5** | Strong moments but the anchor is implicit, or visuals are clean but not memorable. |
| **3/5** | Competent and clear but safe. Nothing the buyer will remember tomorrow. |
| **2/5** | Generic category deck. Interchangeable with competitors. |
| **1/5** | Template deck. No voice, no anchor, no moment. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** The gold standard deck had multiple standout moments: an emotional anchor indicting the buyer's own staffing plan as structurally doomed, a revenue-leaking visual that made the invisible visible, and a closing line dividing labor between the operator and the platform that the champion would quote in their next leadership meeting. The deck had a distinct voice -- empathetic, direct, operator-to-operator. No competitor could put their logo on this deck without it sounding wrong.
- **Weak (1-2):** A template deck with stock photography, generic headlines ("Leading the Future of [Category]"), and a voice indistinguishable from the category. The buyer sits through it, says "thanks, we'll be in touch," and never opens the PDF again. The champion has nothing to forward -- or worse, they forward it and it makes them look like every other vendor pitch.

## What a Perfect Deck Looks Like

A 5/5 across all six dimensions means:

1. **Headlines alone tell the complete story in sequence.** A partner who reads only slide titles understands the full argument -- problem, stakes, solution, proof, ask.
2. **All five stages in order with an earned reveal.** Problem stage builds pain -> failed alternatives -> stakes. The solution enters as relief, not a pitch. Proof validates. Close is specific.
3. **Every slide makes one point, understandable in seconds. ~10 content slides.** Nothing crammed. Nothing redundant. The team chose what matters most.
4. **Every solution claim maps to an established pain. No orphans.** The buyer can trace every capability back to a problem they just felt. Every problem raised gets resolved.
5. **Specific, mechanistic proof with named customers and a demo moment.** Metrics trace to product mechanisms. Logos are from the buyer's peer set. The skeptic has nothing left to question.
6. **Clear emotional anchor, a memorable moment the buyer will repeat, and a distinct voice.** The champion forwards this deck to their boss without editing it.

The gold standard deck from the corpus -- a home services AI platform (Series B) -- is the worked example: ~10 content slides, headlines that tell the story alone, a Problem stage that builds pain through a failed alternative to escalating stakes, an earned reveal, explicit problem/solution mirroring, mechanistic proof with named case studies, and an emotional anchor around the structural futility of the buyer's default plan that makes the audience feel their approach is the enemy.

**The total score range is 6-30.** Use this guide:

| Score | Assessment |
|-------|------------|
| **27-30** | Production-ready. Deck can run first meetings without revision. |
| **22-26** | Strong foundation. One or two dimensions need tightening. |
| **16-21** | Structural gaps. Arc or focus problems will lose the room. |
| **10-15** | Needs rework. The narrative isn't carrying the meeting. |
| **6-9** | Start over from the POV. The deck has no story to tell. |

## Common Pitch Deck Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Label Headlines Instead of Claim Headlines

**Weak:** "The Problem" / "Our Solution" / "Why Us" / "Market Overview"
**Strong:** Headlines that name the specific pain, indict the default plan, and close with a clear division of labor between the buyer and the product.

Labels tell the audience what category of information is coming. Claims make the argument. The headline is the most valuable real estate on the slide -- the one line every audience member reads, even the one checking their phone. Using it for a label is like paying for a billboard and writing "Advertisement" on it.

### Mistake 2: Unearned Reveal -- Product Appears After One Problem Slide

The deck spends one slide on the problem and immediately introduces the product. The audience hasn't felt enough pain to want the cure. An earned reveal requires at least two Problem-stage beats: the core pain, plus either failed alternatives or escalating stakes. The gold standard deck spends three slides before the reveal. A well-structured framework maps to three problem slides before the solution. One problem slide is never enough.

### Mistake 3: Skipping Failed Alternatives

The deck names the problem but never indicts the buyer's current approach. Without a moment that names the buyer's default plan and explains why it structurally can't work, the buyer thinks "I'll just hire more people" and the deck hasn't answered their most natural objection. Failed alternatives do two things: they preempt the buyer's default plan and they make the reveal feel inevitable by closing off the exits. Skipping this beat means the solution competes with the buyer's imagination.

### Mistake 4: Solution Section as Feature Tour

The solution slides list capabilities -- "AI-powered call handling," "real-time analytics," "automated workflows" -- without connecting each one to a pain the deck established. This is a feature tour, not a narrative resolution. The buyer heard about revenue leakage and growth ceilings but is now being shown a product spec. The fix: every solution slide must explicitly resolve a specific pain from the Problem stage. Explicit mirroring -- where each problem beat is matched by a corresponding resolution -- is the model.

### Mistake 5: Proof Before Solution, or Generic Logo-Wall Proof

Two related failures. Proof before solution means the audience sees evidence for claims they haven't heard yet -- they don't know what the metrics are proving. Generic proof means the audience sees a logo wall and "Trusted by 500+ companies" but has no mechanism, no named outcomes, and no case story. The fix: place proof after solution, make metrics specific and traceable (not "10x ROI" but a specific dollar amount tied to a product mechanism), and include at least one named customer with a real outcome.

### Mistake 6: Sprawl -- 15+ Content Slides

The deck tries to cover every feature, every segment, every use case. The narrative loses focus. The meeting runs long. The audience remembers the length, not the argument. Sprawl signals that the team couldn't decide what matters most -- which means the strategy isn't clear. A 10-slide deck that makes the argument is stronger than a 20-slide deck that covers everything. The discipline is in what you cut.

### Mistake 7: No Emotional Anchor -- Competent, Clear, and Completely Forgettable

The deck is well-structured, the claims are accurate, the proof is real. But there's no moment the buyer will carry out of the room. No named enemy. No memorable visual. No line the champion will quote to their boss. The deck is a vendor overview, not an argument. The fix isn't adding animation or clever design -- it's finding the emotional anchor. What is the buyer's default plan that will fail? Name it. Dramatize it. Make the buyer feel caught. The strongest decks have a single line -- often in the failed alternatives slide -- that the buyer remembers for weeks because it names the thing they've been doing and explains why it will never work.

## How This Rubric Differs From Other Document Types

This rubric evaluates pitch decks. Other document types require different rubrics:

- **Messaging Framework rubric** evaluates structural completeness, pillar construction, buyer specificity, clarity & specificity, and competitive clarity. A framework is a reference architecture; a deck is a delivery format. See [[concepts/messaging-framework-rubric]].
- **POV Narrative rubric** evaluates opening hook, arc integrity, resolution & vision, writing quality & flow, and overall narrative strength. A POV is a long-form persuasive story; a deck compresses that story into slides. See [[concepts/pov-rubric]].
- **Homepage rubric** evaluates hero clarity, audience mapping, problem clarity, value clarity, competitive POV, and proof & social validation. A homepage is a compressed positioning artifact for cold visitors; a deck is a narrative delivery format for a known audience. See [[concepts/homepage-rubric]].
