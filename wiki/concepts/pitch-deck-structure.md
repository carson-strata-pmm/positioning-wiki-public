# Pitch Deck Structure

## Definition

A pitch deck is the narrative translation of the messaging framework into a presentation format. It is not a feature demo, a product walkthrough, or a slide-by-slide data dump -- it is a structured argument that moves the audience from "I have this problem" to "this company is the answer." The pitch deck follows the same problem-to-proof arc as the messaging framework but compresses it into 10-15 slides designed for a live conversation.

## How It Works

### Narrative Deck vs. Feature Demo Deck

| Narrative Pitch Deck | Feature Demo Deck |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Opens with the buyer's problem | Opens with the product's capabilities |
| Builds an argument (problem → solution → proof) | Walks through screens and workflows |
| Creates urgency before introducing the product | Assumes urgency exists |
| Product enters as the inevitable answer | Product enters as the subject |
| Ends with "why us" and a clear ask | Ends with a pricing slide |

Most B2B companies default to feature demo decks because they're easier to build -- the product team already has screenshots and specs. But narrative decks outperform demo decks in early-stage sales conversations because they do the work of creating urgency, establishing category context, and differentiating before the prospect ever sees the product.

### The Standard Pitch Deck Arc

The Strata messaging framework maps directly onto a pitch deck structure. Each framework layer corresponds to a deck section:

```
Slides 1-3: THE PROBLEM
    Hero problem → Root causes → Consequence cascade
    (Framework Layers 2-3: Hero Problem + Pain Pillars)

Slides 4-5: THE MARKET CONTEXT
    Why now → What's changing → Why old approaches fail
    (Framework Layer 1: POV / Opening Strategy)

Slide 6: THE SOLUTION
    Product introduction → Category positioning
    (Framework Layer 4: Solution Introduction)

Slides 7-9: THE VALUE
    Value pillar 1 → Value pillar 2 → Value pillar 3
    (Framework Layers 5-7: Value Pillars + USPs + Outcomes)

Slides 10-11: THE PROOF
    Customer metrics → Use cases → Logos
    (Framework Layer 11: Proof Points)

Slide 12: WHY US
    Differentiation → Competitive positioning
    (Framework Layer 9: Differentiation)

Slide 13: THE ASK
    CTA → Next steps → Pricing (if appropriate)
```

### How Each Framework Layer Maps to Slides

**Slides 1-3: The Problem.** The hero problem anchors the opening. Slide 1 names the tension. Slide 2 unpacks the root causes (why the problem is structural, not behavioral). Slide 3 quantifies consequences -- this is where $294K figures, "70% of OPEX on spreadsheets," and "90% of leads gone in 20 seconds" land. The problem section should make the audience uncomfortable with the status quo before any solution is introduced.

**Slides 4-5: The Market Context.** This is the POV in slide form. Why is the problem worsening? What structural shift makes it urgent now? Eudia's "matter volume up 79% with flat budgets" works here. Agency's "more people = worse CX" paradox works here. The market context validates that the problem isn't the buyer's fault -- the world changed.

**Slide 6: The Solution.** The "hinge" slide. After 5 slides of problem and market context, the product enters as the inevitable answer. Name the category first, then the product. "This is why we built the first headcount management platform" (TeamOhana). "Meet Kai -- a superintelligent customer AI agent" (Agency). The solution slide should feel like a relief, not a pitch.

**Slides 7-9: The Value.** One slide per value pillar. Each slide follows the same structure: value pillar name (headline), 2-3 USPs (supporting evidence), outcome statement (what the buyer gets). Avoca's "Book more jobs" slide would include: AI answers every call in <3 seconds, automated lead follow-up, outbound nurture campaigns → "Never miss a call. Full board year-round."

**Slides 10-11: The Proof.** This is where metrics, customer logos, quotes, and case studies live. The proof section validates everything claimed in slides 7-9. Strongest when it includes named customers with hard numbers: "My Plumber Plus saw 13% revenue growth" (Avoca), "$294K saved in unapproved headcount costs" (TeamOhana).

**Slide 12: Why Us.** The differentiation slide. Structured comparison against alternatives. TeamOhana's three-axis differentiation (vs. spreadsheets, vs. FP&A/HRIS, vs. point solutions) translates directly into a comparison table. Eudia's "Traditional Law Firm vs. Augmented Law Firm" 10-dimension table is a pitch deck slide waiting to happen.

**Slide 13: The Ask.** Clear next step matched to the conversation stage. Not a generic "let's chat" -- a specific action: "pilot with one team," "see your data in the platform," "meet the team behind Kai."

### The Problem-First Principle

The most important structural decision in a pitch deck is that the product doesn't appear until slide 6. Half the deck is spent on the problem, the market, and the urgency. This mirrors the messaging framework's architecture: the hero problem and pain pillars come before the solution introduction.

Why this works: by the time the product appears, the audience has already agreed on three things:
1. The problem is real (hero problem + root causes)
2. The problem is costly (consequence cascade)
3. The problem is structural, not solvable by trying harder (market context)

The product enters as the resolution to a tension the audience already feels. This is fundamentally different from a feature deck, where the product enters on slide 1 and the audience spends the next 12 slides wondering "but do I have this problem?"

## Examples from Client Work

### Strongest Pitch Deck Mapping: TeamOhana

TeamOhana's framework translates to a pitch deck almost verbatim:
- **Slide 1:** "The Headcount Headache" -- 70% of OPEX managed on spreadsheets
- **Slide 2:** Root causes -- finance tools weren't built for recruiting; recruiting tools weren't built for finance
- **Slide 3:** $294K in unapproved costs, 75% of time wasted on manual processes
- **Slide 4-5:** Cross-functional gap is structural -- more tools won't fix the category mismatch
- **Slide 6:** "The first and only headcount management platform"
- **Slides 7-9:** Real-time visibility → Unified process → Automated workflows
- **Slide 10:** $294K savings, 75% reduction, customer testimonial
- **Slide 11:** Three-axis differentiation table
- **Slide 12:** Ask

Every slide draws directly from the framework. No creative leaps needed.

### Strongest Proof Slides: Avoca

Avoca has the richest proof point library, which makes slides 10-11 unusually powerful:
- Named customers: My Plumber Plus, Aire Serv
- Hard metrics: 13% revenue growth, 8.6x increase in bookings, $800K from a single branch
- Operational proof: 75% booking rate, <3 second answer time, near-zero abandonment

Most B2B pitch decks have a "logos" slide and hope for the best. Avoca can build a proof section that closes.

### Most Complex Deck Decision: Eudia

Eudia's dual-narrative architecture means two different pitch decks for two different audiences:
- **CLO deck:** Opens with "Speed. Risk. Speed. Risk." Problem is legal throughput and cost. Proof is $30M+ in identified opportunities.
- **ALF deck:** Opens with "Disruption of my career." Problem is existential career threat. Proof is the 10-dimension comparison table.

The strategic sequencing ("CLOs must be sold first") determines which deck gets used when.

### Pitch Deck Without Proof: Agency

Agency's framework is structurally sound but has a proof point gap -- no named customers or hard metrics. The pitch deck would be strong through slide 9 (problem → solution → value) but weak on slides 10-11 (proof). This is the most common pitch deck failure mode: a compelling narrative that can't close because the proof section is thin.

## Patterns & Lessons

- **The framework writes the deck.** If the messaging framework is strong, the pitch deck is a translation exercise, not a creative one. If building the deck requires inventing new claims or restructuring the argument, the framework has gaps.
- **Problem slides are the most important.** Audiences forgive weak proof slides if the problem framing is sharp. They don't forgive strong proof slides if the problem framing is generic. Invest disproportionately in slides 1-3.
- **One value pillar per slide.** The three-pillar structure maps perfectly to three slides. Cramming all three onto one slide destroys the narrative pacing. Each pillar gets its own headline, USPs, and outcome.
- **Proof gaps are pitch deck killers.** The gap between a strong narrative (slides 1-9) and weak proof (slides 10-11) is where deals stall. This is why proof point maturity matters -- see [[concepts/proof-point-patterns]].
- **The deck should be speakable.** Every slide headline should work as a sentence the presenter actually says. "Book more jobs" works. "Value Pillar 2: Customer Experience Optimization" doesn't.
- **Differentiation slides need structure.** A bullet list of "why we're better" is weaker than a structured comparison table. TeamOhana's three-axis approach and Eudia's 10-dimension table give the audience a framework for evaluating, not just a list of claims.
