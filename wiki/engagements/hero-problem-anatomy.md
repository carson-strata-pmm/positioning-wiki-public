# Hero Problem Anatomy

_Deep analysis of problem framing patterns across all 10 completed client engagements_

## Overview
This article analyzes the hero problem framing section across all client engagements in the Strata wiki, identifying structural commonalities, recurring archetypes, language patterns, quality tiers, and a universal formula that emerges from the data.

## Structural Commonalities

Every hero problem in the wiki, regardless of industry or category stance, follows a three-layer structure:

### Layer 1: A named tension the buyer lives with daily
The problem isn't introduced as "your software is bad" or "you need a better tool." It's introduced as a tension between two things the buyer already cares about that they've been forced to trade off against each other:

| Client | Tension |
|--------|---------|
| Eudia | Speed vs. risk |
| Agency | Scale vs. customer intimacy |
| Jane | Patient care vs. running a business |
| Unity Games | Creative ambition vs. operational overhead |
| Unity Industry | 3D design capability vs. 3D collaboration access |
| General Legal | Legal need vs. affordability of expertise |
| Avoca | Growth ambition vs. front-office capacity |
| TeamOhana | Headcount spending (70% OPEX) vs. process maturity to manage it |
| BrightEdge | Content creation capability vs. discoverability |
| Snappy | Recognition intent vs. gifting execution |

This is universal. Every single hero problem can be restated as: **"You are forced to choose between X and Y, and that trade-off is costing you more than you realize."**

### Layer 2: A structural reason the tension exists
The problem isn't bad luck or bad management -- it's architectural. The system itself creates the tension:

- **Agency:** The human-led model creates handoffs and bureaucracy by design
- **Eudia:** The legal operating model was built for a slower world
- **TeamOhana:** Finance tools weren't built for recruiting; recruiting tools weren't built for finance
- **General Legal:** The billable hour model concentrates expertise by design
- **Avoca:** The CSR workforce is structurally volatile (seasonal, high-churn)
- **Unity Industry:** 3D tools require specialized expertise by design, locking out non-experts

Structural framing makes the problem feel inevitable rather than incidental. The buyer can't fix it by trying harder -- they need a different architecture.

### Layer 3: A consequence cascade the buyer is underestimating
Every hero problem connects to a consequence the buyer either hasn't measured or has learned to tolerate:

- **Avoca:** ~$46K per CSR churn event, 90% of leads lost after 20 seconds
- **TeamOhana:** $294K average in unapproved headcount costs
- **Eudia:** Matter volume rising 79% with flat budgets
- **Agency:** The 80-90% "long tail" of customers that go unserved
- **General Legal:** Everyday decisions made without legal judgment
- **Snappy:** Inequitable gifting experiences eroding engagement

The consequence doesn't have to be financial -- Jane's consequence is personal ("work eats into your nights," "you feel isolated") -- but it must be specific enough to feel measurable.

## Problem Archetypes

Five distinct archetypes emerge from the 10 hero problems:

### Archetype A: The Paradox
**Pattern:** The thing you're doing to solve the problem is actually making it worse.

| Client | Paradox |
|--------|---------|
| Agency | Hiring more people to improve CX degrades CX |
| Eudia | Accelerating business pace makes legal risk worse, not better |

**Signature language:** "paradox," "irony," "the more you... the less you..."
**When it works:** Sophisticated buyers who can recognize systemic contradictions. Category creators.

### Archetype B: The Gap
**Pattern:** Two things that should be connected are separated -- and the gap costs more than anyone realizes.

| Client | Gap |
|--------|-----|
| Unity Industry | "The Reality Gap" -- 3D design vs. flat collaboration |
| Unity Games | "The Success Gap" -- creative work vs. operational overhead |
| TeamOhana | The gap between FP&A and HRIS that headcount falls into |

**Signature language:** "gap," "disconnect," "misalignment," "between"
**When it works:** When the product bridges two existing categories or workflows. Works for both category creators and redefininers.

### Archetype C: The Hidden Cost
**Pattern:** You're tolerating an enormous cost because you've never measured it.

| Client | Hidden Cost |
|--------|-------------|
| Avoca | Revenue leakage from missed calls, dropped leads, CSR churn -- compounding invisibly |
| TeamOhana | 70% of OPEX managed through untrusted spreadsheet processes |

**Signature language:** "leakage," "hidden," "invisible," numbers that shock (70%, $294K, $46K)
**When it works:** Operational buyers (VP Ops, Controller, CFO) who respond to quantified waste. Head-to-head and category creators alike.

### Archetype D: The Constraint
**Pattern:** Something valuable is artificially scarce, and the scarcity itself is the problem.

| Client | Constraint |
|--------|-----------|
| General Legal | Elite legal expertise is rationed because the model concentrates it |
| BrightEdge | Discoverability is scarce because AI is rewriting the rules of search |

**Signature language:** "constraint," "scarcity," "reserved for," "rationed," "ill-equipped"
**When it works:** When the product unlocks access to something previously gatekept. Category redefininers especially.

### Archetype E: The Identity Burden
**Pattern:** The buyer's professional identity is being split between what they love and what they're forced to do.

| Client | Burden |
|--------|--------|
| Jane | "You're not just the clinician -- you're the admin, the owner, the systems department" |

**Signature language:** "overwhelmed," "feel," "you're not just," emotional verbs (dread, exhaust, isolate)
**When it works:** Owner-operators and solo buyers where the personal and professional are inseparable. The most emotionally resonant archetype but the narrowest in applicability.

**Note:** Snappy doesn't cleanly fit one archetype. "Ad hoc gifting" is part hidden cost (treating gifting as a checkbox), part constraint (lack of expertise and bandwidth). It's the weakest hero problem in the corpus.

## Language Patterns

### The "The ___" naming formula
7 of 10 hero problems use a definite article + noun/noun phrase. The definite article implies the problem already exists and is waiting to be named -- not invented by the vendor. It creates discovery, not persuasion.

### Tension words
A cluster of words recur across hero problems to signal structural conflict: gap, paradox, ratio, dual, headache, constraint, ceiling. These are all words that imply a force holding something back -- a limit, a contradiction, a barrier. None of them are product words. They're physics words, economics words, body words.

### Second-person address
Avoca and Jane both address the buyer directly: "Your growth is hitting a ceiling," "The dual responsibility... can feel overwhelming." This is used exclusively for owner-operator personas where business pain and personal pain are the same thing.

### Compounding language
Multiple hero problems use language that implies the problem gets worse over time without intervention: "compounds until growth stalls" (Avoca), "the more people you invest... the more challenging it becomes" (Agency), "matter volumes rise, budgets stay flat" (Eudia), "every new hire amplifies spreadsheet chaos" (TeamOhana). This creates urgency without FUD -- the problem isn't scary because something might go wrong, it's scary because the current trajectory is already going wrong.

## Quality Tiers

### Tier 1: Complete on all three layers
| Client | Hero Problem | Why it's strong |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| Agency | "The Customer Value Paradox" | The name itself is the argument. Self-contained: you don't need context to understand it. |
| Eudia | "The speed-to-risk ratio" | Mathematically precise. "Ratio" implies it can be measured and optimized -- natural bridge to the product. |
| TeamOhana | "The Headcount Headache" | Alliterative, visceral, universally felt. Easiest to say aloud, most likely to travel through an organization. |

**What they share:** The name alone tells the story.

### Tier 2: Strong framing, softer name
| Client | Hero Problem | What holds it back |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| Unity Industry | "The Reality Gap" | Abstract without the narrative. Requires setup. |
| General Legal | "The Scarcity Constraint" | Intellectually precise but doesn't trigger emotion. |
| Avoca | "Your growth is hitting a ceiling" | Strong because it's personal, but it's a sentence, not a name. |

### Tier 3: Framing without structural depth
| Client | Hero Problem | What's missing |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Unity Games | "The Success Gap" | Could describe almost any B2B problem. Not specific enough to own. |
| Jane | "The dual responsibility" | Emotionally powerful in context but reads as a section header, not a named problem. |
| BrightEdge | "Discoverability in the AI era" | Descriptive but not a name. A topic, not a tension. |
| Snappy | "Ad hoc gifting" | Weakest in the corpus. Describes status quo without naming a tension, cost, or surprise. |

## The Universal Formula

Every strong hero problem follows this structure:

> **Name the tension** (2-4 words) that exists because **the system is architected wrong** (structural cause), and it's **costing more than you think** (consequence cascade).

```
HERO PROBLEM = Named Tension + Structural Cause + Hidden Consequence
```

### The quality test
1. **The name test:** Can you say the name to a stranger in the buyer's role and have them lean in?
2. **The inevitability test:** Does the problem feel structural and worsening, not situational?
3. **The pre-product test:** Does the problem resonate before the buyer knows your product exists?

### Archetype selection guide

| If the buyer's situation is... | Use this archetype | Model client |
|-------------------------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Their solution is making things worse | Paradox | Agency |
| Two things that should be connected are disconnected | Gap | Unity Industry, TeamOhana |
| They're tolerating a cost they've never measured | Hidden Cost | Avoca, TeamOhana |
| Something valuable is artificially restricted | Constraint | General Legal |
| Their identity is being split by competing demands | Identity Burden | Jane |

The strongest hero problems in the corpus (Agency, Eudia, TeamOhana) combine two archetypes. The layering is what gives them depth.
