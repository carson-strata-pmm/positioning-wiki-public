# Problem Architecture

## Definition

Problem architecture is the full structural stack that makes a B2B narrative's problem feel real, urgent, and unsolvable by the buyer's current approach. It goes beyond the hero problem name to include the root causes that explain *why* the problem exists structurally and the consequence cascade that quantifies *what's at stake*. The hero problem alone creates recognition; the full architecture creates conviction.

## How It Works

Every Strata engagement builds a three-layer problem stack:

```
Layer 1: Hero Problem (the named tension -- 2-4 words)
    |
Layer 2: Root Causes (2-3 structural reasons the problem exists)
    |
Layer 3: Consequence Cascade (2-3 specific, preferably quantifiable costs)
```

This structure then decomposes fractally into three pain pillars, each replicating the same three-layer pattern at a lower level.

### Layer 1: The Hero Problem (Named Tension)

The hero problem is a compact, memorable label for a systemic challenge. It compresses a complex trade-off into something the buyer instantly recognizes. See [[concepts/hero-problem-framing]] for the full naming framework.

The hero problem always names a tension between two things the buyer cares about:

| Client | X | Y | Named Tension |
|--------|---|---|---------------|
| Eudia | Speed | Risk | "The speed-to-risk ratio" |
| Agency | Scale | Customer intimacy | "The Customer Value Paradox" |
| TeamOhana | Headcount spending | Process maturity | "The Headcount Headache" |
| General Legal | Legal need | Affordability | "The Scarcity Constraint" |
| Jane | Patient care | Running a business | "The dual responsibility" |

**Why the name alone isn't enough:** "The Headcount Headache" tells the buyer *what* the problem is. But it doesn't tell them *why* it exists or *what it costs*. Without layers 2 and 3, the hero problem is a bumper sticker -- recognizable but not actionable.

### Layer 2: Root Causes (Why the Problem Exists Structurally)

Root causes explain why the problem can't be solved by trying harder, hiring more, or using existing tools. The problem is *architectural* -- the system itself creates the tension.

**The "trying harder" test:** If the buyer could solve the problem by being more disciplined, the cause isn't structural enough. Structural causes point to the operating model, the tool landscape, or the market structure.

| Client | Hero Problem | Root Causes |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Agency | "The Customer Value Paradox" | The human-led model creates handoffs and bureaucracy by design. More people = more coordination overhead = worse CX. |
| TeamOhana | "The Headcount Headache" | Finance tools weren't built for recruiting. Recruiting tools weren't built for finance. The gap between categories is the problem. |
| Eudia | "The speed-to-risk ratio" | The legal operating model was built for a slower world. Matter volume rises but budgets stay flat. |
| Avoca | "Your growth is hitting a ceiling" | The CSR workforce is structurally volatile -- seasonal, high-churn, expensive to train. Every departure resets the customer experience. |
| General Legal | "The Scarcity Constraint" | The billable hour model concentrates expertise by design. Elite judgment is rationed to those who can afford hourly rates. |
| Unity Industry | "The Reality Gap" | 3D tools require specialized expertise, locking out non-experts. Designs live in formats that most stakeholders can't open or interact with. |

**The indictment pattern:** Root causes in the strongest frameworks (Agency, Eudia, TeamOhana) don't just explain the problem -- they *indict* the existing approach. They show that the buyer's current tools/processes/vendors fail for architectural reasons, not feature reasons. This is what creates the opening for a new approach.

### Layer 3: Consequence Cascade (What's at Stake)

Consequences quantify the cost the buyer has been tolerating because they assumed it was the cost of doing business. Making the invisible visible creates the "lean in" moment.

| Client | Consequence | Type | Strength |
|--------|------------|------|----------|
| Avoca | ~$46K per CSR churn event | Financial | Strong -- specific, surprising |
| Avoca | 90% of leads gone after 20 seconds | Operational | Strong -- vivid, measurable |
| TeamOhana | $294K average in unapproved headcount costs | Financial | Strong -- specific, quantified |
| TeamOhana | 70% of OPEX managed on spreadsheets | Operational | Strong -- shocking percentage |
| Eudia | Matter volume up 79% with flat budgets | Operational | Strong -- industry data |
| Agency | 80-90% of enterprise customers underserved | Revenue | Strong -- invisible cost |
| Jane | "Work eats into your nights," "you feel isolated" | Personal | Moderate -- emotional but not quantified |
| Snappy | Inequitable gifting experiences | Cultural | Weak -- vague, not measured |

**The visibility test:** The best consequences are ones the buyer hasn't measured. A specific number ($294K) is more powerful than a big number ("millions in waste"). A vivid statistic (90% of leads gone in 20 seconds) is more powerful than an abstract claim ("lead leakage").

**Financial consequences are strongest** but not always available. When hard numbers don't exist, use industry statistics (Eudia's "79% of legal teams saw matter volume increase") or emotional consequences (Jane's identity-level pain). But push for quantification whenever possible.

### The Fractal Pattern: Pillars as Mini Problem Architectures

The hero problem decomposes into three pain pillars, and each pillar replicates the same three-layer structure:

```
Pain Pillar Name (2-4 words)
    |
    Pain Statement (one sentence -- the mini hero problem)
    |
    Causes (2-3 structural reasons this sub-problem exists)
    |
    Consequences (2-3 specific costs)
```

**TeamOhana's "Untrusted data" pillar (cleanest example):**
- **Pain statement:** Headcount data is fragmented, error-ridden, and stale
- **Causes:** Scattered data (across tools), bad data (manual entry errors), outdated data (no real-time sync)
- **Consequences:** No visibility into true headcount position, security risk from shared spreadsheets

This fractal quality means the audience can engage at any level: the hero problem for the elevator pitch, the pillars for the sales deck, the full cause-consequence stacks for the deep dive.

## Examples from Client Work

### Strongest Problem Architecture: TeamOhana

```
Hero Problem: "The Headcount Headache"
  Root Cause: Finance tools ≠ recruiting tools; 70% of OPEX managed via spreadsheets
  Consequence: $294K in unapproved costs, 75% of time wasted on manual processes

  Pillar 1: Untrusted data (visibility)
    Causes: Scattered, bad, outdated data
    Consequences: No visibility, security risk

  Pillar 2: Lack of control (governance)
    Causes: No approval process, no guardrails
    Consequences: Unapproved hires, budget overruns

  Pillar 3: Wasted time (productivity)
    Causes: Manual reconciliation, data chasing
    Consequences: 60 hrs/month wasted, slow decisions
```

Every layer is tight: no overlap, no gaps, specific consequences at every level.

### Weakest Problem Architecture: Snappy

```
Hero Problem: "Ad hoc gifting"
  Root Cause: No strategic function owns gifting; treated as a checkbox
  Consequence: Inequitable experiences (vague)

  Pillar 1: Ad hoc gifting (restates hero problem -- not a decomposition)
  Pillar 2: Gifting operations (thin -- one cause)
  Pillar 3: Recipient impact (vague consequences -- "gifts don't land")
```

P1 restates the hero problem rather than decomposing it. Consequences are vague throughout. The architecture lacks the specificity that creates urgency.

## Patterns & Lessons

- **The hero problem is necessary but not sufficient.** The name creates recognition; the architecture creates conviction. A named tension without root causes is an observation. Without consequences, it's not urgent.
- **Root causes must be structural, not behavioral.** "The team isn't disciplined enough" is a behavioral cause. "The tools weren't built for this workflow" is a structural cause. Only structural causes justify a new product.
- **Quantified consequences close deals.** Avoca's "$800K from a single branch's outbound campaign" and TeamOhana's "$294K in unapproved costs" are the kind of numbers that move procurement. Most clients have proof point gaps at the consequence layer -- this is the most common weakness.
- **The fractal pattern enables multi-level selling.** The same problem architecture supports the C-suite conversation (hero problem + consequences), the VP conversation (pillars + causes), and the practitioner conversation (full cause-consequence stacks). One architecture, three levels of detail.
- **Problem architecture predicts messaging quality.** The engagements with the tightest problem architecture (TeamOhana, Avoca, Agency) also have the strongest value pillars, the clearest differentiation, and the most usable copy banks. Weak architecture cascades into weak everything.
