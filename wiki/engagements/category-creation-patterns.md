# Category Creation Patterns

_Cross-client analysis of Agency, Eudia, and TeamOhana_

## Overview
Three Strata clients took a category creation positioning stance: **Agency** (Superintelligent Customer AI Agent), **Eudia** (Augmented Intelligence Platform + Augmented Law Firm), and **TeamOhana** (Headcount Management Platform). This article identifies the patterns that emerge across their hero problems, opening strategies, and positioning playbooks.

## Hero Problem Patterns

All three hero problems share two key traits:

### 1. They indict a structural system, not a product gap
None of them say "you need better software." Instead:
- **Agency's "Customer Value Paradox"** -- the human-led CX model itself is broken
- **Eudia's "Speed-to-risk ratio"** -- the fundamental tension of legal work is unsustainable
- **TeamOhana's "Headcount Headache"** -- the cross-functional process for managing 70% of OPEX is held together by spreadsheets

### 2. They describe a problem that exists before any category exists to solve it
This is critical for category creation -- the hero problem must feel real and urgent in a world where the buyer doesn't yet know there's a category for the solution. Each problem names a gap between existing categories (FP&A vs. HRIS, bolt-on AI vs. autonomous AI, people/software/outside counsel).

### 3. The naming is compact and emotionally loaded
"Paradox," "Headache," and "ratio" are all words that compress complexity into something visceral and repeatable. Two of three use alliteration or rhythm ("Customer Value Paradox," "Headcount Headache").

## Opening Strategy Patterns

Each category creator uses a different primary opening move, but they converge on a shared structure:

| Client | Primary Opening | Secondary Opening |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Agency | **Name a paradox** -- more people = worse CX | Invoke an inevitable future ("the future isn't more people -- it's Kai") |
| Eudia | **Indict the status quo** -- old solutions all have trade-offs | Name a paradox (speed vs. risk) + Invoke an inevitable future ("lead or be left behind") |
| TeamOhana | **Surface a hidden cost** -- 70% of OPEX on spreadsheets, $294K in unapproved costs | (Simpler, single-move opening) |

### Key pattern: category creators layer multiple opening strategies
Agency and Eudia both combine 2-3 moves. The layering works because category creation requires more rhetorical work -- you need to destabilize the buyer's existing mental model before you can install a new one. TeamOhana is the exception; its opening is simpler because the problem is more universally felt (everyone hates headcount spreadsheets) and requires less worldview-shifting.

### All three end with "inevitable future" energy
Even if not all use it as a formal opening. Agency says the future is AI agents, not people. Eudia says lead or be left behind. TeamOhana implies the spreadsheet era is ending. This makes sense: category creation requires the buyer to believe the world is changing, not just that they have a problem.

## The Shared Playbook

Across all three, the category creation positioning follows this sequence:

1. **Name a systemic problem that no existing category solves** -- the problem lives in the gap between categories
2. **Indict existing approaches as structurally incapable** -- people, legacy software, and incumbents all fail for architectural reasons, not feature reasons
3. **Draw a bright line** between old and new -- "bolt-on AI vs. AI-native" (Agency), "artificial intelligence vs. augmented intelligence" (Eudia), "spreadsheets/platforms vs. purpose-built" (TeamOhana)
4. **Name the new category** and position the client as its first and only occupant
5. **Require a named enemy** -- every category creator defines what it is not as clearly as what it is

## Category Creators vs. Head-to-Head Competitors

The strongest differentiator: category creators build their hero problem around a paradox or structural failure in the market itself, while head-to-head competitors build theirs around operational pain the buyer already recognizes. Category creators have to teach the buyer something new; head-to-head competitors just have to prove they're better.
