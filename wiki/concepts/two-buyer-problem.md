# The Two-Buyer Problem

## Definition

The two-buyer problem is the strategic challenge that nearly every B2B product faces: the product must resonate with multiple stakeholders who have different priorities, different pain, and different decision criteria -- but the narrative can only lead with one. Trying to serve both buyers equally produces messaging that resonates with neither. The solution is to designate a primary buyer who anchors the narrative and a secondary buyer who is served without diluting it.

## How It Works

### Why Most B2B Products Have Two Buyers

B2B buying decisions almost never involve a single person. Across 11 Strata engagements, 9 have at least two distinct buyer roles, and 3 have three or more. The two-buyer dynamic typically takes one of three forms:

| Form | Description | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Economic + Champion** | One person controls the budget; another champions the product internally | Eudia: CLO (economic buyer) + Head of Legal Ops (influencer/champion) |
| **Cross-Functional Pair** | Two leaders from different functions must both adopt for the product to work | TeamOhana: VP Finance + Head of Talent; Numeric: Controller + CFO |
| **Demand + Supply** | Two sides of a marketplace or ecosystem require separate narratives | Eudia: CLO (demand) + Law Firm Partner (supply) |

### How Messaging Fails When It Serves Both Equally

The most common mistake is writing messaging that splits the difference between two buyers. The result:

- **The hero problem is generic.** It describes a broad organizational challenge rather than a specific buyer's pain. Nobody reads it and thinks "that's *my* problem."
- **The pillars are unfocused.** Some address Buyer A's pain, others address Buyer B's, and the overall structure feels scattered rather than building a single argument.
- **The language is neutral.** Instead of speaking directly to one buyer's world, it uses corporate language that offends no one and moves no one.

> **LoneScale (homepage corpus):** Before repositioning, the homepage "addressed a problem with no clear owner" and was "unclear who the product was for or what it actually did." After repositioning, it had a "clear focus on a use case someone owns."

The homepage corpus repeatedly shows that weak positioning is *too wide* -- trying to address multiple buyers simultaneously. Strong positioning is *narrower but deeper* -- focused on one buyer, one problem, one category.

### The Primary/Secondary Buyer Framework

**Step 1: Designate the primary buyer.**
This is the person whose pain anchors the hero problem, whose language shapes the messaging, and whose decision triggers the deal. They are not always the economic buyer -- sometimes the champion is primary because they drive the evaluation.

**Step 2: Build the narrative for the primary buyer.**
The hero problem, pain pillars, value pillars, and opening strategy are all built around how the primary buyer experiences the problem. The primary buyer should read the messaging and think "this company understands my world."

**Step 3: Serve the secondary buyer without diluting.**
The secondary buyer gets:
- A dedicated section in the ICP portion of the framework
- Persona-specific language within value pillars (not separate pillars)
- Proof points and use cases that address their specific concerns
- Optionally, a separate page or section (not a separate narrative)

## Examples from Client Work

### Pattern 1: Primary + Secondary (Same Narrative, Different Emphasis)

**Avoca** -- Three buyer tiers, one narrative:
- **Primary:** Owner-Operator (CEO, GM, President) -- business pain and personal pain are inseparable. Messaging uses second-person address, empathetic tone, operational language.
- **Secondary:** Customer Service Leader (Director of CS) -- manages call center performance. Cares about staffing, training, consistency.
- **Tertiary:** Digital Transformation Lead (CTO) -- PE-backed portfolios standardizing tech.

The framework is built for the owner-operator. The hero problem ("Your growth is hitting a ceiling") speaks directly to them. Pillars are framed around their operational reality (missed calls, inconsistent CX, staffing challenges). The secondary and tertiary buyers are served through persona-specific language in the ICP section and sales enablement materials, not through a diluted narrative.

**General Legal** -- Stage-based buyer shift:
- **Primary (Scale segment):** General Counsel at scaling companies -- mounting legal demand without resources
- **Primary (Growth segment):** Founder/CEO at early-stage companies -- needs legal guidance but can't justify in-house

Rather than writing a single narrative that splits the difference, General Legal segments by stage. The same hero problem ("The Scarcity Constraint") resonates with both, but the consequences and language shift: the GC feels "consumed by operational work," while the CEO feels "flying blind on legal risk."

### Pattern 2: Dual Narrative (Separate Frameworks)

**Eudia** -- The most complex two-buyer engagement in the corpus:
- **Narrative 1 (CLO):** Demand side. Hero problem: "The speed-to-risk ratio." Three pillars about legal throughput, spend, and team capacity.
- **Narrative 2 (ALF/Partners):** Supply side. Hero problem: "Disruption of my career." Three pillars about client trust, career constraints, and being left behind.

These are fundamentally different people with fundamentally different motivations. The CLO cares about speed and cost. The law firm partner cares about career survival and professional identity. No single narrative could serve both.

**The strategic sequencing:** Eudia's Messaging Map explicitly states "CLOs must be sold first." The demand side drives the initial go-to-market. Only after CLOs adopt the platform do they redirect outside counsel spend toward Augmented Law Firms. The two narratives are connected but sequenced.

### Pattern 3: Multi-Persona Framework (One Framework, Multiple Persona Sections)

**Unity Games** -- Three buyer personas within one framework:
- **Developers:** Care about reliability, iteration speed, creative flow
- **UA/Advertisers:** Care about player acquisition in a post-IDFA world
- **Monetization/Publishers:** Care about sustainable revenue across platforms

The platform story ("develop, deploy, grow") is unified, but each value pillar contains persona-specific language. "Develop without compromise" speaks to developers. "Grow on your terms" speaks to monetization leaders. The hero problem ("The Success Gap") is broad enough to cover all three but is primarily framed through the developer's experience.

### The Cross-Functional Pair: A Special Case

When two buyers from different functions must *both* adopt the product, the hero problem should name the gap *between* their functions:

| Client | Buyer A | Buyer B | Hero Problem Names the Gap |
|--------|---------|---------|---------------------------|
| TeamOhana | VP Finance / FP&A | Head of Talent / Recruiting | "The Headcount Headache" -- the gap between finance and recruiting processes |
| Eudia | CLO (demand) | Law Firm Partner (supply) | Two separate hero problems for two sides of the marketplace |
| Numeric | Controller | CFO | "The Accounting Gap" (candidate) -- the gap between closing the books and understanding them |

**TeamOhana's approach is the model:** The hero problem names the cross-functional gap itself (headcount managed by disconnected teams). Neither Finance nor Talent "owns" the problem alone -- the problem *is* the disconnect between them. This gives both buyers a reason to care without favoring either.

## Patterns & Lessons

- **Designate one primary buyer, always.** Even in dual-narrative engagements (Eudia), one narrative is primary for go-to-market sequencing. "CLOs must be sold first" is a primary buyer decision.
- **The primary buyer's pain shapes the hero problem.** The hero problem must use language the primary buyer would use. If the primary buyer wouldn't say "paradox," don't use "paradox" (see [[concepts/hero-problem-framing]] on language matching).
- **Cross-functional pairs need a "gap" hero problem.** When two buyers must co-adopt, the hero problem should name the structural gap between their functions, not one buyer's pain.
- **Dual narratives are rare and expensive.** Only Eudia required two separate messaging frameworks. Most multi-buyer engagements can be served with one framework plus persona-specific sections.
- **Homepage = primary buyer only.** The homepage should speak to the primary buyer. Secondary buyers get their own landing pages. Trying to address multiple buyers on the homepage is the most common homepage positioning failure (see [[concepts/homepage-structure]]).
- **Academic/skeptical buyers need different language.** Eudia explicitly defines voice as "academic, insider, inventive" because lawyers reject generic SaaS copy. The buyer's sophistication level shapes how the POV is expressed.
