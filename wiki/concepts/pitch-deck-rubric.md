# Pitch Deck Rubric

## Definition

A scoring rubric for evaluating the quality of a B2B SaaS pitch deck. This rubric produces an overall score out of 10, a high-level summary, eight graded narrative sections, and a deck-wide focus & density check. The overall score is driven by arc integrity -- how well the deck follows the proven story arc that moves the audience from "I have this problem" to "this company is the answer."

## What This Rubric Is For

This rubric evaluates **pitch decks** -- the narrative delivery format that compresses the POV into slides and delivers it live or async. A pitch deck has one job: to move the audience from "I have this problem" to "this company is the answer" in roughly 10 content slides.

A pitch deck is not a messaging framework. It is not a POV narrative. It is not a homepage. It is graded on narrative arc, slide-level clarity, and emotional impact -- not structural completeness (messaging framework criteria), long-form writing quality (POV criteria), or page architecture (homepage criteria). Each document type has different evaluation criteria because each serves a different function:

- A **pitch deck** is a narrative delivery format. It must carry the full argument across a sequence of slides, each making exactly one point, with headlines that tell the story alone. It is graded on arc integrity, opening insight, problem clarity, consequences, failed alternatives, solution ownership, proof quality, and close strength.
- A **messaging framework** is a structured reference architecture. It is graded on structural completeness, pillar construction, buyer specificity, and competitive clarity -- criteria that are irrelevant for a deck.
- A **POV narrative** is a persuasive story. It is graded on opening hook, arc integrity, and emotional momentum -- criteria that overlap with but are distinct from deck evaluation.
- A **homepage** is a compressed expression of the framework. It is graded on above-the-fold clarity, page architecture, and CTA design.

The reference standard: a strong initial pitch deck is roughly 10 content slides. The corpus gold standard is a home services AI platform (Series B) whose headline sequence alone tells the full story -- moving from the daily pain of revenue leakage, through the structural failure of the buyer's default staffing plan, to the growth ceiling consequence, and finally introducing the AI front office as the inevitable resolution, closing with a division of labor that leaves the operator focused on their craft.

## Positioning Stance -- Read This First

Every review must account for which positioning stance the asset is pursuing. The stance dictates what "good" looks like -- the same section can demand opposite execution depending on the stance. Before evaluating any section, determine which stance applies.

### Stance 1: Creating a New Category

- **Reference point:** a job-to-be-done of your choosing
- **Competitive alternative:** the status quo way people do that JTBD (NOT named vendors)
- **Target customer:** people doing the JTBD you selected, who don't know a category exists
- **What good looks like:** indicts the status quo approach/architecture, not competitors. Names a new category. Educates the buyer that the problem is real and unsolved. Invokes an inevitable future. Longest game, highest risk, highest reward.
- **Stance error to flag:** naming or comparing against competitor brands. A category creator who benchmarks against named vendors is undermining their own category -- they're implicitly admitting they're in someone else's category.

### Stance 2: Competing in a Mature Category

- **Reference point:** the category name itself (the category already exists in buyers' minds)
- **Competitive alternative:** other named vendors in the category
- **Target customer:** people already shopping the category
- **What good looks like:** sharp, specific differentiation against named competitors. Clear reason-to-choose-us within an understood frame. Mind space and budget already exist -- the job is to win the comparison, not create the need.
- **Stance error to flag:** failing to differentiate against named competitors, or trying to educate the buyer on a problem they already understand. In a mature category, spending the asset explaining why the problem matters is wasted -- the buyer knows. Vague "we're better" claims without specific head-to-head contrast also fail.

### Stance 3: Competing in an Immature Category

- **Reference point:** the job-to-be-done of the category
- **Competitive alternative:** the status quo way people do that JTBD
- **Target customer:** people doing the JTBD who may not know the category exists yet
- **What good looks like:** balances light category education with differentiation. Positions to become a leader as the category solidifies. Some teaching required, but not from scratch. Indicts the status quo while gesturing at the emerging category.
- **Stance error to flag:** positioning as if the category is mature (over-indexing on competitor comparison when buyers don't yet know the category) OR positioning as if creating from scratch (over-educating when an emerging category already has some awareness). The immature stance requires calibration between the two.

## How Stance Changes This Evaluation

The positioning stance shifts what each section should look for:

- **Arc Integrity:** A category creator's deck needs more Opening Insight, Problem, and Consequences real estate to build the category -- the audience must be educated on why the category needs to exist before the solution can land. Expect 3-4 slides before the reveal. A mature-category deck can move faster through the problem stages (the buyer already knows the category) and allocate more slides to differentiation and proof. An immature-category deck needs moderate problem-stage investment -- enough to frame the emerging category, not so much that it feels like a lecture.

- **Opening Insight:** For a category creator, the opening must immerse the buyer in the status quo JTBD pain -- the buyer may not yet know a better way exists. For a mature-category player, the opening can assume the buyer already feels the problem and open with a provocative reframe or surprising data. For an immature-category player, the opening should surface the JTBD pain while hinting that an emerging solution category exists.

- **Failed Alternatives:** For a category creator, failed alternatives indict the status quo approaches (not named vendors) as architecturally incapable. For a mature-category player, failed alternatives contrast named competitors structurally. For an immature-category player, failed alternatives indict both the status quo and the limitations of early category entrants.

- **The Solution:** For a category creator, the solution slides must resolve the status quo pains, not competitive pains -- the mirror is "old JTBD approach" vs. "new category approach." For a mature-category player, the solution slides should explicitly contrast with how named competitors solve each pain. For an immature-category player, the solution mirrors the status quo failure while also gesturing at why this approach is superior to early category entrants.

- **Proof & Validation:** For a category creator, proof must demonstrate that the new category works -- early adopter wins and outcome metrics that validate the category's viability. Named-vendor comparison metrics are a stance error. For a mature-category player, proof should include head-to-head wins, migration stories, and competitive benchmarks. For an immature-category player, proof should demonstrate both category viability and company leadership.

## Stance-Execution Mismatch

Before scoring, determine whether the asset's actual execution matches the stance the user selected. A common and costly error is choosing one stance strategically but executing as if pursuing another. Examples:

- Selected "creating a category" but the asset is full of head-to-head competitor comparisons (executing as mature-category)
- Selected "mature category" but the asset spends most of its energy educating the buyer on a problem they already understand (executing as category creation)
- Selected "immature category" but positions with no category education at all (executing as mature) or pure greenfield education (executing as creation)

When you detect a mismatch, this is one of the most valuable findings you can surface. Name it explicitly. Explain what stance the execution actually reflects versus what they selected. Then guide them to commit to their chosen direction -- help them stay the course. The goal is not to tell them which stance is right; it's to help them execute consistently with the strategic direction they've chosen. A confused stance produces confused positioning.

## The Proven Story Arc

Arc integrity is the spine of this rubric. It is not a standalone section -- it is the organizing logic behind the overall /10 score. The deck is measured against the proven story arc:

**Opening Insight -> Problem -> Consequences of Inaction -> Failed Alternatives -> The Solution You Own -> Proof & Validation -> Close**

The overall score heavily reflects how well the deck follows this arc in this order. Strong decks earn the reveal -- by the time the solution appears, the audience viscerally wants a way out. Weak decks skip beats, reorder the arc, or front-load the company before earning attention.

### Common Arc Violations

When a deck violates the arc, the HIGH-LEVEL SUMMARY must call it out specifically and in plain language -- naming exactly where the deck deviates and why the proven arc works better.

**Opening with the company, the team, or a logo/customer wall instead of a customer insight.** This is the most common and most damaging arc violation. Strong decks open by dropping the buyer into a customer-centric insight or tension -- leading with yourself loses the room before you've earned attention. Company credibility and logos belong later, as proof, not as the opening. The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) opened with a statement about the buyer's daily reality of revenue leakage -- a claim about the buyer's world, not about the company. A deck that opens with "About Us," team bios, or a wall of customer logos has already failed the arc before the argument begins.

**Jumping to the solution before establishing the problem.** The deck introduces the product on slide 2 or 3 without building tension. The audience hasn't felt enough pain to want the cure. The gold standard deck spends three slides building pain before the solution appears. One problem slide is never enough.

**Showing proof before the solution is introduced.** Proof before solution tries to build credibility before the audience knows what they're evaluating. Logos and metrics before the product is explained feel disconnected -- the audience doesn't know what the proof is proving.

**No clear consequences of inaction so the problem feels optional.** The deck names a problem but never quantifies or dramatizes what happens if it goes unsolved. Without stakes, the buyer thinks "interesting but not urgent." The problem must feel expensive and risky to ignore.

**Skipping failed alternatives so the solution feels unearned.** Without a moment that names and indicts the buyer's default plan, the buyer's first instinct remains unchallenged. Failed alternatives close off the exits and make the reveal feel inevitable. Skipping this beat means the solution competes with the buyer's imagination.

**No close / no ask.** The deck builds the full argument and then just stops. No next step, no specific ask, no vision that lands. A deck without a close is a conversation without a purpose.

## The Slide Taxonomy

Every slide in a pitch deck serves a specific function within the narrative arc. This is the genomic sequence model -- the taxonomy of slide types a deck draws from, organized by stage:

### Context Stage
- **Company credibility intro** -- who we are and why we're credible (used sparingly; avoid opening with this)
- **Team** -- founders, advisors, domain expertise
- **Agenda / meeting setup** -- what we'll cover today (optional, often verbal)
- **Audience-centric discovery** -- "what we learned about your world" (powerful when drawn from real research)
- **Strategic context / market shift** -- what changed in the world that makes this urgent now
- **Unique insight / POV** -- the company's proprietary point of view on the market

### Problem Stage
- **Core problem** -- "why buy anything" -- the hero problem slide
- **Emotional anchor / enemy** -- the "bogeyman" slide that dramatizes the villain (optional but actively rewarded)
- **Stakes / negative consequences** -- what happens if the problem goes unsolved
- **Urgency trigger** -- "why act now" -- temporal pressure, regulatory change, competitive threat
- **Failed alternatives** -- what the buyer has already tried and why it can't work

### Solution Stage
- **Platform reveal** -- the "hinge" slide where the product enters as the inevitable answer
- **Differentiation** -- "why buy us" -- what makes this approach structurally different
- **Capabilities proof** -- how the product actually works (mechanism, not feature list)
- **Solution overview** -- the full platform at a glance
- **Applied benefits / use cases** -- the product applied to the buyer's specific workflows
- **Platform validation / architecture** -- technical credibility for technical buyers
- **Adoption path / maturity model** -- how implementation works and what the ramp looks like

### Proof Stage
- **Business case / ROI** -- quantified financial impact with named mechanisms
- **Vision fit / future state** -- what the buyer's world looks like after adoption
- **Social proof / customer logos** -- peer validation from recognizable companies
- **Analyst validation** -- third-party credibility (Gartner, Forrester, etc.)
- **Case proof / customer story** -- a named customer with a specific, traceable outcome

### Close Stage
- **Call to action / next steps** -- specific ask matched to the conversation stage

Not every deck needs every slide type. A 10-slide deck selects the right subset for the audience and stage. But the stages must appear in order: **Context -> Problem -> Solution -> Proof -> Close.** A deck that opens with Solution or buries Problem after Proof has a structural failure, not a style preference.

## The Eight Graded Sections

Each section receives a status: **STRONG**, **NEEDS WORK**, or **MISSING**. Each section also receives prose feedback explaining what's present, what's weak or missing, and how to sharpen it. Ground guidance in methodology -- do not write the user's slides for them.

### 1. Arc Integrity

**Status: STRONG / NEEDS WORK / MISSING**

**What to evaluate:**

Does the deck follow the proven story arc in the right order? This section provides the overall structural assessment and names any arc violations.

- **Do the stages appear in order: Opening Insight -> Problem -> Consequences -> Failed Alternatives -> Solution -> Proof -> Close?** This is the structural spine. Decks that open with the solution, the company, or logos before earning attention fail structurally.
- **Does the reveal slide feel earned?** The audience should feel relief when the solution appears -- "finally, someone gets this." If the deck jumps from one problem slide straight to product, the reveal is unearned. The audience hasn't suffered enough to want the cure.
- **Is each headline a claim, not a label?** A headline that names a specific buyer pain is a claim. "The Problem" is a label. Labels waste the most valuable real estate on the slide -- the one line the audience always reads.
- **Does each headline advance the argument -- or repeat the previous slide's point?** Repetition disguised as progression is the most common headline failure.
- **Are the headlines speakable?** Every headline should work as a sentence the presenter actually says out loud. "Value Pillar 2: Customer Experience Optimization" is unspeakable. A headline framing the product as an automated version of the buyer's broken process is speakable.

**Calibration examples:**

- **STRONG:** The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) followed the arc precisely: it opened with a buyer-centric insight about daily revenue leakage, moved to a structural indictment of the buyer's default staffing plan, escalated to the growth ceiling consequence, then introduced the AI front office as the resolution, followed by proof with named customers and hard metrics, and closed with a clean division of labor. The headline sequence alone told the full story. Every headline was a claim. Each advanced the argument. The reveal felt earned because the deck spent three slides making the audience uncomfortable with the status quo.
- **NEEDS WORK:** A deck that follows the general arc but opens with one weak beat -- a single problem slide before jumping to the solution, or a failed alternatives slide that implies rather than explicitly indicts the buyer's default plan. The structure is recognizable but one stage is thin.
- **MISSING:** A deck that opens with "About Us" (company history, team, funding), moves to a product overview, then shows a problem slide as justification. The problem exists to justify the product rather than the product existing to resolve a felt tension. Or a deck with no discernible arc -- a feature tour with a logo slide.

### 2. Opening Insight

**Status: STRONG / NEEDS WORK / MISSING**

**What to evaluate:**

Does the deck open with a customer-centric insight or tension that makes the buyer lean in? The opening is about the customer's world, not the company.

- **Does the first slide drop the buyer into their world -- their problem, their reality, their cost?** Not the company's history. Not a logo wall. Not a team slide. The buyer's world.
- **Is it a genuine insight (teaches the buyer something or reframes what they already know) or a generic observation ("the market is growing")?** An insight makes the buyer think "I never thought of it that way" or "how do they know my life?" A generic observation makes them check their phone.
- **Would the buyer lean in after the first slide -- or are they waiting for the deck to get relevant?** Forward momentum starts on slide 1. If the opening doesn't create tension, nothing that follows can recover the room's attention.

**Calibration examples:**

- **STRONG:** The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) opened by dropping the buyer -- a home services owner-operator -- into a felt pain they experience daily: revenue quietly leaking out of the business. The slide was about the buyer's world, not the company. It surfaced a cost the buyer may not have quantified. The buyer leaned in because the deck knew something about their reality.
- **NEEDS WORK:** A deck that opens with a market statistic ("Home services is a $600B market") -- factually correct but impersonal. The buyer is informed, not hooked. The insight is about the market, not about the buyer's specific operational reality.
- **MISSING:** A deck that opens with the company -- "Founded in 2021, backed by [investors], trusted by [logos]." The buyer has no reason to care about the company before the deck has earned their attention. Credibility slides belong in the proof stage, not the opening.

### 3. Problem

**Status: STRONG / NEEDS WORK / MISSING**

**What to evaluate:**

Is the problem clear, unique, and nameable? Does it indict a system, not a symptom? Could the buyer repeat the problem name after one viewing?

- **Is the problem named -- does it have a specific, ownable framing?** A coined label for the core tension is a name. "Companies face challenges" is not.
- **Does the problem indict a structural system (why it keeps happening) or just describe a symptom (what happens)?** Framing the problem as a structural mismatch between an essential function and a volatile workforce is structural. "You're missing calls" is a symptom.
- **Could the buyer repeat the problem name to a colleague after seeing the deck once?** If the problem isn't memorable enough to travel, the deck hasn't done its job.

**Calibration examples:**

- **STRONG:** The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) indicted the structural mismatch between the front office's importance and its staffing volatility. The problem wasn't a surface symptom like missed calls -- it was that the most critical function in the business ran on the most unstable workforce. The buyer could repeat this framing because it reframed something they already felt.
- **NEEDS WORK:** A deck that names a real problem but frames it as a symptom -- "companies lose revenue from missed calls" -- without explaining the structural reason it keeps happening. The buyer agrees but doesn't feel caught.
- **MISSING:** No problem framing at all. The deck jumps from the opening to the product without establishing why the buyer should care.

### 4. Consequences of Inaction

**Status: STRONG / NEEDS WORK / MISSING**

**What to evaluate:**

Is the cost of the status quo clear and ideally quantified? Does staying put feel expensive and risky, or does the problem feel optional?

- **Are the stakes quantified or at least dramatized?** Specific dollar costs per churn event and lead-response benchmarks make the invisible visible. Unquantified consequences feel theoretical.
- **Does the deck make the buyer feel that doing nothing is the riskiest option?** The consequences should make inaction feel more dangerous than action. If the buyer can comfortably stay on the current path, the deck hasn't created enough urgency.
- **Are consequences specific to the buyer's world -- not generic business risks?** "Revenue growth stalls" is generic. Framing the ceiling as a direct result of operational leakage the buyer can't see is specific.

**Calibration examples:**

- **STRONG:** The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) made the consequence visceral -- the buyer's growth stalls not because of the market but because of operational leakage they can't see. Combined with supporting data on lead response windows and CSR churn costs, the status quo felt untenable. The buyer couldn't un-hear those numbers.
- **NEEDS WORK:** A deck that asserts consequences ("this costs companies millions") without making them specific or traceable to the buyer's own reality. The stakes are stated but not felt.
- **MISSING:** The deck names a problem but never addresses what happens if it goes unsolved. The problem feels like a nice-to-fix, not a must-fix.

### 5. Failed Alternatives

**Status: STRONG / NEEDS WORK / MISSING**

**What to evaluate:**

Are the things buyers have already tried, and why each one falls short, made clear? Without this, the solution feels unearned. Are alternatives indicted structurally (architecturally incapable) not just dismissed?

- **Does the deck name the buyer's default plan and explain why it will never work?** The buyer's first instinct is always "I'll just do more of what I'm doing." The deck must preempt this.
- **Are alternatives indicted architecturally -- not just dismissed as "worse"?** Explaining why hiring more staff is structurally incapable of solving the problem (volatile workforce, linearly scaling costs, no quality improvement) is an architectural indictment. "Competitors are bad" is dismissal, not indictment.
- **Does closing off the exits make the reveal feel inevitable?** After failed alternatives, the audience should feel like there's no way back to the status quo. The solution enters as the only remaining path.

**Calibration examples:**

- **STRONG:** The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) named the buyer's default plan -- hiring more front-office staff -- and explained why it was structurally incapable of solving the problem: the workforce is inherently volatile, costs scale linearly, and quality doesn't improve with headcount. The buyer's Plan A was dead. The reveal felt inevitable.
- **NEEDS WORK:** A deck that mentions alternatives ("some companies try hiring more staff") but doesn't explain the structural reason they fail. The alternative is noted but not indicted.
- **MISSING:** The deck jumps straight from the problem to the solution. The buyer's most natural objection ("I'll just hire more people" / "I'll use the tool I already have") goes unanswered. The solution competes with the buyer's imagination.

### 6. The Solution

**Status: STRONG / NEEDS WORK / MISSING**

**What to evaluate:**

Is the solution introduced as unique and genuinely owned by this company -- something only they can credibly claim? Does it resolve the specific problem and consequences established earlier? Is it mechanism, not feature list?

- **Does the solution enter as the inevitable answer to the tension the deck just built?** The reveal should feel like relief, not a pitch. Introducing the AI front office as the resolution of the three-slide tension the deck built is the model.
- **Does every solution claim map back to a pain the deck established?** Orphan capabilities (features with no corresponding problem) signal feature-selling, not problem-solving. Orphan pains (problems raised but never resolved) undermine credibility.
- **Is the solution explained as mechanism (how it works architecturally) or feature list (what it does)?** Explaining that AI handles every call in under three seconds because it runs the front office end-to-end is mechanism. "AI-powered call handling" is a feature.
- **Could only this company credibly make this claim?** The logo-swap test: could a competitor put their name on this solution section? If yes, the solution isn't owned.

**Calibration examples:**

- **STRONG:** The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) mirrored the problem explicitly. The deck established four specific revenue leaks (missed calls, lost follow-ups, unworked estimates, after-hours gaps) and the solution resolved each one: AI answers every call, automated follow-up sequences, estimate nurture campaigns, 24/7 coverage. The buyer could draw a line from each pain to its resolution. No orphans on either side. The mechanism was clear -- AI handles the front office end-to-end, not just one channel.
- **NEEDS WORK:** A deck whose solution is directionally right but framed as a feature tour -- "AI-powered call handling, real-time analytics, automated workflows" -- without connecting each capability to a specific pain the deck established. The buyer heard about revenue leakage but is being shown a product spec.
- **MISSING:** No clear solution introduction. The deck shows product screenshots or a demo without ever framing why this approach is different or how it resolves the specific tensions built earlier.

### 7. Proof & Validation

**Status: STRONG / NEEDS WORK / MISSING**

**What to evaluate:**

Is there credible, specific proof AFTER the solution? Mechanistic metrics, a real case study with named outcomes, a demo/in-action moment, peer-relevant logos? Does proof validate the specific claims made?

- **Does proof come after the solution, where it validates claims already made?** Proof before solution tries to build credibility before the audience knows what they're evaluating.
- **Are metrics specific and mechanistic?** Hundreds of thousands in after-hours revenue captured per customer and thousands of outbound jobs won from campaign activity are specific and traceable to the product's mechanism. "10x ROI" with no mechanism is marketing math the buyer can't verify.
- **Is there a case study or customer story with named outcomes -- not just a logo wall?** A logo wall says "companies use us." A named case study with a specific, traceable growth metric says something the buyer can evaluate. The second is proof. The first is decoration.
- **Is there a demo or "in action" moment that makes the product real?** At some point the audience needs to see or feel the product working. Without it, the product remains abstract.
- **Are logos from the right buyer segment -- companies the prospect would recognize as peers?** A deck targeting home services owner-operators with enterprise SaaS logos has a segment mismatch.
- **Does proof address the skeptic's question ("does this actually work?") not just the believer's ("how big can this get?")?** Vision slides serve believers. Proof slides serve skeptics. Most rooms have both.

**Calibration examples:**

- **STRONG:** The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) featured named customers from the target segment, hard metrics tied to specific mechanisms (double-digit revenue growth, order-of-magnitude increase in bookings, significant campaign-attributed revenue from a single branch), operational proof (high booking rate, sub-three-second answer time, near-zero abandonment), all from businesses the prospect recognized as peers. Every metric traced back to a product capability established in the solution section. The skeptic in the room had nothing left to question.
- **NEEDS WORK:** A deck with proof that is real but generic -- a logo wall of recognizable brands with no context, or metrics that aren't tied to the specific claims made ("Trusted by 500+ companies" without explaining what those companies achieved).
- **MISSING:** No proof at all. Or proof from the wrong buyer segment entirely. Or proof placed before the solution is introduced, so the audience doesn't know what the metrics are proving.

### 8. The Close

**Status: STRONG / NEEDS WORK / MISSING**

**What to evaluate:**

Does the deck end well -- a clear next step, ask, or vision that lands? Or does it just stop?

- **Is there a specific ask matched to the conversation stage?** "Pilot with one team" is specific. "Let's chat" is not. The close must give the buyer a concrete next action.
- **Does the close feel like the natural conclusion of the arc -- not a tacked-on afterthought?** The best closes connect back to the opening tension. The gold standard deck closed with a division of labor that circled back to the growth ceiling -- the buyer focuses on what they're good at while the platform handles the front office that was holding them back.
- **Is there a memorable closing line the champion will carry into their next internal meeting?** The champion saw the pitch. Now they need to sell internally. The close gives them the language to do it.

**Calibration examples:**

- **STRONG:** The gold standard deck from a home services AI platform (Series B) closed with a line that divided labor between the operator and the platform -- functioning as both an ask and a vision. It resolved the opening tension (growth hitting a ceiling because the buyer was trying to do everything), gave the champion a repeatable line, and made the next step feel obvious.
- **NEEDS WORK:** A deck that ends with a generic "Next Steps" slide listing "Schedule a demo / Visit our website / Contact us." Functional but forgettable. The champion has nothing to quote.
- **MISSING:** The deck just stops. No ask, no next step, no closing vision. The audience finishes and thinks "so... what now?"

## Deck-Wide Check: Focus & Density

This is a separate deck-wide assessment, not one of the eight graded sections.

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does each slide make exactly ONE point?** Supporting elements are fine -- a problem slide can make one point (revenue leaks) with four supporting examples of where leaks happen. But the slide must have a single takeaway. If someone asked "what was that slide about?" there should be one answer, not two.
- **Is the deck roughly 10 content slides?** Initial pitch decks that sprawl past ~12 content slides lose focus and signal an unclear narrative. If the team couldn't choose what to cut, they haven't clarified what matters most. (Appendix or extra slides for deep-dive questions don't count against this.)
- **Can each slide be understood in under 10 seconds of looking?** Show the slide to someone for 10 seconds, then hide it. Can they tell you the point? If not, the slide is too dense.
- **Is there any slide that tries to be two slides -- problem AND solution, or capabilities AND proof?** Combination slides are always weaker than two focused slides. They force the audience to process two ideas simultaneously and remember neither.

**Calibration examples:**

- **Focused:** A 10-slide deck where each slide has one headline, one point, and supporting evidence that reinforces that single point. The audience never has to choose where to look or what to remember.
- **Sprawling:** A 20-slide deck that tries to cover every product feature, every customer segment, every use case. Slides carry competing messages. The deck runs 45 minutes because the narrative can't focus. The audience remembers the length, not the argument. Sprawl past ~12 content slides signals an unclear narrative.

## Scoring the Overall /10

The overall score is a single number out of 10 that reflects the deck's quality as a narrative argument. **Arc integrity gates the top of the range** -- a deck that violates the core arc (e.g., opens with the company, skips consequences, shows proof before the solution) cannot score above a ceiling even if individual slides are polished. You cannot polish your way past a broken arc.

### How to translate sections into the overall score

1. **Start with arc integrity.** How well does the deck follow the proven arc in the right order? This sets the ceiling.
2. **Evaluate each section.** How many sections are STRONG vs. NEEDS WORK vs. MISSING?
3. **Weight arc violations heavily.** A deck with all eight sections present but in the wrong order scores lower than a deck with seven sections in the right order.
4. **Apply the focus & density check.** A sprawling deck or slides that cram multiple points reduce the score.

### Band Guide

| Score | Assessment |
|-------|------------|
| **9-10** | Production-ready. Arc is clean, every section is STRONG or nearly so, focus is tight. Deck can run first meetings without revision. |
| **7-8** | Strong with gaps. Arc is intact but one or two sections NEED WORK. The argument lands but specific beats are thin. |
| **5-6** | Structural problems. Arc has one or more violations (wrong order, missing stages) or multiple sections NEED WORK / are MISSING. The deck loses the room at identifiable moments. |
| **3-4** | Needs rework. Arc is broken -- deck opens with the company, jumps to solution before building pain, or has multiple MISSING sections. Individual slides may be polished but the narrative doesn't carry. |
| **1-2** | Start over. No discernible arc. Feature tour or company overview. The deck has no story to tell. |

### Arc violation ceilings

- A deck that opens with the company/team/logos instead of a customer insight: **cannot score above 6** regardless of slide quality.
- A deck that jumps to the solution before establishing the problem: **cannot score above 5**.
- A deck with no consequences of inaction and no failed alternatives: **cannot score above 6** (the problem feels optional and the solution feels unearned).
- A deck that shows proof before the solution is introduced: **cannot score above 5**.
- A deck with three or more MISSING sections: **cannot score above 4**.

## Common Pitch Deck Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Opening with the Company Instead of a Customer Insight

**Weak:** Slide 1 is "About Us" with founding year, investor logos, team headshots, or a customer logo wall.
**Strong:** Slide 1 names a specific buyer pain -- a statement about the buyer's world.

Opening with the company is the most common arc violation and the most damaging. It loses the room before the deck has earned attention. The buyer hasn't been given a reason to care about who you are yet. Company credibility and logos belong in the proof section, after the argument has been made. Strong decks open by dropping the buyer into a customer-centric insight or tension.

### Mistake 2: Label Headlines Instead of Claim Headlines

**Weak:** "The Problem" / "Our Solution" / "Why Us" / "Market Overview"
**Strong:** Headlines that name the specific pain, indict the default plan, and close with a clear division of labor between the buyer and the product.

Labels tell the audience what category of information is coming. Claims make the argument. The headline is the most valuable real estate on the slide -- the one line every audience member reads, even the one checking their phone. Using it for a label is like paying for a billboard and writing "Advertisement" on it.

### Mistake 3: Unearned Reveal -- Product Appears After One Problem Slide

The deck spends one slide on the problem and immediately introduces the product. The audience hasn't felt enough pain to want the cure. An earned reveal requires at least two problem-stage beats: the core pain, plus either failed alternatives or escalating stakes. The gold standard deck spends three slides before the reveal. A well-structured framework maps to three problem slides before the solution. One problem slide is never enough.

### Mistake 4: Skipping Failed Alternatives

The deck names the problem but never indicts the buyer's current approach. Without a moment that names the buyer's default plan and explains why it structurally can't work, the buyer thinks "I'll just hire more people" and the deck hasn't answered their most natural objection. Failed alternatives do two things: they preempt the buyer's default plan and they make the reveal feel inevitable by closing off the exits. Skipping this beat means the solution competes with the buyer's imagination.

### Mistake 5: Solution Section as Feature Tour

The solution slides list capabilities -- "AI-powered call handling," "real-time analytics," "automated workflows" -- without connecting each one to a pain the deck established. This is a feature tour, not a narrative resolution. The buyer heard about revenue leakage and growth ceilings but is now being shown a product spec. The fix: every solution slide must explicitly resolve a specific pain from the problem stage. Explicit mirroring -- where each problem beat is matched by a corresponding resolution -- is the model.

### Mistake 6: Proof Before Solution, or Generic Logo-Wall Proof

Two related failures. Proof before solution means the audience sees evidence for claims they haven't heard yet -- they don't know what the metrics are proving. Generic proof means the audience sees a logo wall and "Trusted by 500+ companies" but has no mechanism, no named outcomes, and no case story. The fix: place proof after solution, make metrics specific and traceable (not "10x ROI" but a specific dollar amount tied to a product mechanism), and include at least one named customer with a real outcome.

### Mistake 7: Sprawl -- 15+ Content Slides

The deck tries to cover every feature, every segment, every use case. The narrative loses focus. The meeting runs long. The audience remembers the length, not the argument. Sprawl signals that the team couldn't decide what matters most -- which means the strategy isn't clear. A 10-slide deck that makes the argument is stronger than a 20-slide deck that covers everything. The discipline is in what you cut.

### Mistake 8: No Emotional Anchor -- Competent, Clear, and Completely Forgettable

The deck is well-structured, the claims are accurate, the proof is real. But there's no moment the buyer will carry out of the room. No named enemy. No memorable visual. No line the champion will quote to their boss. The deck is a vendor overview, not an argument. The fix isn't adding animation or clever design -- it's finding the emotional anchor. What is the buyer's default plan that will fail? Name it. Dramatize it. Make the buyer feel caught. The strongest decks have a single line -- often in the failed alternatives slide -- that the buyer remembers for weeks because it names the thing they've been doing and explains why it will never work.

## How This Rubric Differs From Other Document Types

This rubric evaluates pitch decks. Other document types require different rubrics:

- **Messaging Framework rubric** evaluates structural completeness, pillar construction, buyer specificity, clarity & specificity, and competitive clarity. A framework is a reference architecture; a deck is a delivery format. See [[concepts/messaging-framework-rubric]].
- **POV Narrative rubric** evaluates opening hook, arc integrity, resolution & vision, writing quality & flow, and overall narrative strength. A POV is a long-form persuasive story; a deck compresses that story into slides. See [[concepts/pov-rubric]].
- **Homepage rubric** evaluates hero clarity, audience mapping, problem clarity, value clarity, competitive POV, and proof & social validation. A homepage is a compressed positioning artifact for cold visitors; a deck is a narrative delivery format for a known audience. See [[concepts/homepage-rubric]].

## Output Note for the Review Engine

This rubric uses a pitch-deck-specific output shape that differs from the other rubrics' scorecard format. The review engine should output:

- **overall_score** (integer, 1-10) -- the single arc-driven score
- **high_level_summary** (string) -- names arc violations explicitly, calls out the biggest structural issue first, and explains in plain language where the deck deviates from the proven arc and why that arc works better
- **sections** (array of objects) -- eight entries, each with:
  - **section_name** (string) -- one of: "Arc Integrity", "Opening Insight", "Problem", "Consequences of Inaction", "Failed Alternatives", "The Solution", "Proof & Validation", "The Close"
  - **status** (string) -- one of: "STRONG", "NEEDS WORK", "MISSING"
  - **feedback** (string) -- prose explaining what's present, what's weak or missing, and how to sharpen it
- **focus_density_check** (string) -- the deck-wide assessment of slide count, single-point discipline, and density

When the review is generated, the selected stance must be passed to the model and factored into every section assessment and the overall score. Company stage may be collected for data but does NOT affect the evaluation -- do not let stage influence scoring or feedback.
