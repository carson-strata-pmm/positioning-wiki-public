# Point of View

## Definition

A Point of View (POV) is the strategic narrative that anchors a company's entire go-to-market story. It is not a product explainer, a mission statement, or a tagline -- it is an opinionated argument about what's broken in the market and why the world must change. The POV comes before all other messaging because it establishes the worldview that makes the product feel inevitable.

## How It Works

### What a POV Is (and Is Not)

| POV | NOT a POV |
|-----|-----------|
| An opinionated argument about the market | A description of what the product does |
| Starts with a truth the buyer already feels | Starts with the company's founding story |
| Names a systemic problem | Lists product features |
| Creates urgency through structural framing | Creates urgency through discounts or timelines |
| Makes the product feel inevitable | Makes the product feel available |

**A POV is not a mission statement.** A mission statement says what the company aspires to be. A POV says what's broken in the world and why the old way of doing things is no longer viable. Mission statements are aspirational and inward-facing. POVs are provocative and outward-facing.

> **Eudia's mission:** "We exist to enable humanity to achieve its highest good through Augmented Intelligence."
> **Eudia's POV:** "Speed. Risk. Speed. Risk. The legal operating model was built for a slower world. AI is how tomorrow's corporate legal teams will transcend the speed-to-risk ratio. You can lead the way or be left behind."

The mission tells you what Eudia wants. The POV tells you what's broken and why you should care.

### Why POV Comes Before Messaging

The POV sits at Layer 1 of the Strata messaging hierarchy -- above the hero problem, above the pillars, above the copy bank. It governs everything below it because it establishes:

1. **The worldview** -- how the company sees the market
2. **The enemy** -- what's broken and who/what is responsible
3. **The stance** -- what the company believes must change
4. **The tone** -- how the company speaks (opinionated, human, strategic)

Without a POV, messaging becomes descriptive rather than persuasive. The team can say what the product does but not why it matters. Every messaging framework in the Strata corpus that has a strong POV (Eudia, Unity Industry, Agency) produces sharper pillars, stronger copy, and more differentiated positioning.

### The Structure of a Strong POV

From the Harmonic-style POV framework used in Strata engagements, a strong POV follows this narrative arc:

```
Human truth → Rising pressure → Systemic failure → Consequences →
Named problem → New worldview → Inevitable solution → Emotional resolution
```

In practice, this compresses into three structural moves:

**Move 1: Open with a truth the buyer lives with daily.**
Not jargon, not abstraction -- a simple, emotionally true observation about the buyer's world.

> **Unity Industry:** "Seeing is believing. It's how we interpret reality and make sense of the world around us."
> **Eudia CLO:** "Speed. Risk. Speed. Risk." (rhythmic repetition that physically embodies the tension)

**Move 2: Name the systemic problem and its consequences.**
This is the hero problem, deployed in narrative form. The problem must feel structural and worsening, not situational.

> **Agency:** "We reflexively hire more people as we grow to serve our customers. But the truth is, the more people you invest... the more challenging it becomes to deliver the experience those customers need."
> **Avoca:** "~$46K in impact per churn event. Revenue leakage from missed calls is invisible until measured."

**Move 3: Paint the future and position the product as inevitable.**
The "hinge moment" -- the narrative shifts from diagnosis to possibility. The product enters not as a pitch but as the natural answer.

> **Unity Industry:** "We're not just powering an industrial revolution. We're leading a revolution in the industrial world."
> **Eudia:** "AI is how tomorrow's corporate legal teams will transcend the speed-to-risk ratio. You can lead the way or be left behind."

### The Five Opening Moves

The POV's opening move sets the rhetorical strategy for the entire narrative. Strata uses five repeatable moves:

| Move | What It Does | Best For | Model Client |
|------|-------------|----------|--------------|
| **Indict the Status Quo** | Frames current approach as structurally broken | Established markets with clear incumbents | Eudia, Agency, General Legal |
| **Name a Paradox** | Reveals a hidden contradiction in the buyer's strategy | Sophisticated buyers; category creators | Agency, Eudia |
| **Surface a Hidden Cost** | Quantifies what the buyer doesn't realize they're losing | Operational buyers (VP Ops, CFO) | Avoca, TeamOhana |
| **Reframe the Market** | Redefines the landscape so the buyer sees it differently | Category redefininers | BrightEdge, Unity Industry |
| **Invoke an Inevitable Future** | Presents coming change as unstoppable | Category creators with AI/tech thesis | Eudia, Agency |

**Most strong POVs combine 2-3 moves.** Eudia's POV combines all three: indicts the status quo (old solutions have trade-offs), names the paradox (speed vs. risk), and invokes the inevitable future (lead or be left behind). TeamOhana uses a simpler single-move opening (hidden cost) because the problem is universally felt and doesn't require worldview-shifting.

## Examples from Client Work

### Strong POVs

**Eudia (CLO) -- Most sophisticated in the corpus:**
Opens with rhythmic repetition ("Speed. Risk. Speed. Risk.") to physically embody the tension. Combines three opening strategies. Introduces the "Iron Man suit" metaphor. Closes with an inevitable future. This POV works because it respects the buyer's intelligence (lawyers are skeptical) while creating genuine urgency.

**Unity Industry -- Most literary:**
Opens with a philosophical observation ("Seeing is believing") and builds to a contrast: gaming has bleeding-edge 3D tools, but industries building critical products are stuck in PowerPoint. Culminates with: "Because if you can't see what you're building, you can't believe in it." This POV works because it taps into something universal -- the gap between imagination and shared understanding.

**Agency -- Most paradox-driven:**
Names the "Customer Value Paradox" directly. Opens by making the buyer's own strategy feel counterproductive. Draws a sharp line between "bolt-on AI" and "AI-native." This POV works because the paradox is immediately recognizable -- every CX leader has felt the diminishing returns of adding headcount.

### Weak or Missing POVs

**Snappy:** No standalone POV. The messaging framework opens with a problem description ("ad hoc gifting") but doesn't take an opinionated stance on the market. The framework describes the status quo without arguing that it must change.

**BrightEdge:** The POV reframes the market (search is becoming conversational) but doesn't fully commit to a stance. "Discoverability in the AI era" is a topic, not an argument. Compare this to Eudia's "lead or be left behind" -- one describes a trend, the other demands a response.

**Jane:** The opening is deeply empathetic ("You're not just the clinician -- you're the admin, the owner") but stays at the identity level without escalating to a structural argument about why the practice management market is broken. It resonates emotionally but doesn't challenge the buyer's worldview.

### POV as a Standalone Document vs. Embedded in the Framework

| Delivery | When to Use | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Standalone POV narrative** | Complex engagements, category creators, multiple audiences | Eudia (CLO POV + ALF POV), Unity Industry ("The Reality Gap") |
| **Embedded in framework** | Simpler engagements, head-to-head competitors | Avoca, TeamOhana, Jane, Snappy |

Eudia's engagement also produced a **Messaging Map** -- a meta-document explaining how the CLO POV, CLO Framework, ALF POV, and ALF Framework relate to each other. This is useful when there are multiple audiences with fundamentally different relationships to the product.

## Patterns & Lessons

- **The POV should not begin with the product.** It should begin with a truth. Usually human. Sometimes operational. Often both. The product enters as the resolution, not the subject.
- **Protect the buyer's dignity.** The buyer is not incompetent. The world changed and the old systems no longer work. Frame it as: "This is not because teams lack talent. They are operating inside systems built for a different era."
- **Make stakes tangible.** Use vivid operational examples instead of abstract claims. Not "organizations struggle with inefficiency." Better: "Calls go unanswered. Deals stall silently. Leaders discover problems too late."
- **The POV should feel inevitable in hindsight.** The best POVs articulate truths the buyer already feels but hasn't named. They are sharper articulations of existing convictions, not externally imposed marketing narratives.
- **POV and framework serve different audiences.** The POV is for internal alignment (whole company). The framework is for marketing/content teams. Conflating them weakens both.
- **Category creators need stronger POVs.** Agency, Eudia, and TeamOhana -- all category creators -- have the strongest POVs. This makes sense: category creation requires more rhetorical work to shift the buyer's mental model.
