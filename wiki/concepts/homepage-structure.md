# Homepage Structure

## Definition

The homepage is the most compressed version of the full messaging narrative. It must accomplish in a single scroll what the messaging framework does across 20+ pages and what the pitch deck does across 13 slides: establish the problem, introduce the solution, prove the value, and convert the visitor. Every homepage section maps to a specific framework layer, and the quality of the homepage is directly proportional to the quality of the underlying framework.

## How It Works

### The Standard Homepage Arc

The homepage follows a predictable narrative arc, validated across 7 Fletch before/after examples and 11 Strata client engagements:

```
Section 1: HERO
    Headline (hero problem or value promise) + subhead + CTA
    → Framework: Hero Problem (Layer 2) or Value Pillars (Layer 5)

Section 2: PROBLEM / CONTEXT
    Why the status quo is broken (1-3 pain points)
    → Framework: Pain Pillars (Layer 3) + POV (Layer 1)

Section 3: SOLUTION INTRODUCTION
    What the product is and what category it belongs to
    → Framework: Solution Introduction (Layer 4)

Section 4: FEATURE SECTIONS
    2-3 sections organized around value pillars, not product modules
    → Framework: Value Pillars (Layer 5) + USPs (Layer 6)

Section 5: PROOF
    Logos, metrics, customer quotes, case studies
    → Framework: Proof Points (Layer 11)

Section 6: CTA
    Clear next step matched to buyer intent
```

### How the Hero Problem Maps to the Hero Section

The hero section headline is the single most important line of copy on the entire website. It has 3-5 seconds to answer the question: "Is this for me?"

**Two headline strategies:**

| Strategy | When to Use | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Problem-first** | When the pain is visceral and the buyer is actively looking for a solution | "Your growth is hitting a ceiling" (Avoca) |
| **Value-first** | When the buyer may not know they have a problem, or the category is aspirational | "The first and only headcount management platform" (TeamOhana) |

The hero section subhead complements the headline: if the headline names the problem, the subhead introduces the solution. If the headline names the value, the subhead names the problem it solves.

**The three-question test:** A strong hero section answers all three within the fold:
1. What problem does this solve?
2. What does this product do?
3. Why is it different?

The homepage before/after corpus consistently shows that weak homepages fail at least one of these three questions. Strong homepages answer all three above the fold.

### How Value Pillars Map to Feature Sections

The feature sections of the homepage should be organized around value pillars, not product modules. This is the most common homepage structural mistake -- organizing by what the product does rather than what the buyer gets.

| Wrong (Product Modules) | Right (Value Pillars) |
|-------------------------|----------------------|
| "Analytics Dashboard" | "Real-time visibility" (TeamOhana) |
| "AI Call Handling" | "Book more jobs" (Avoca) |
| "Workflow Engine" | "Automated workflows" (TeamOhana) |
| "Integration Hub" | "One place to collaborate in 3D" (Unity Industry) |

Each feature section follows the same mini-structure:
- **Headline:** The value pillar name (3-5 words)
- **Body:** 1-2 sentences explaining what the buyer gets
- **Supporting evidence:** 2-3 USPs that prove the value claim
- **Optional:** A metric or outcome statement

The three-pillar structure maps perfectly to homepage feature sections: three sections, each led by a value pillar headline, each supported by USPs. This creates visual rhythm and narrative consistency.

### The Before/After Patterns

The Fletch before/after corpus reveals six recurring change types when homepages are repositioned:

| Change Type | What It Fixes | Example |
|-------------|--------------|---------|
| **Category Repositioning** | No category anchor → explicit category | CommandBar anchored to "digital adoption tools" |
| **Problem Ownership** | Diffuse problem → buyer-specific problem | LoneScale refocused on "a use case someone owns" |
| **POV Addition** | Descriptive page → opinionated stance | CommandBar went from neutral to "highly opinionated" |
| **Concrete Explanation** | Abstract benefits → plain-language mechanism | LoneScale moved from "high-level benefits" to "what it does and how" |
| **Page Architecture** | Too thin or too sprawling → right-sized | Concierge AI expanded from 550px to 3076px; Omnibound compressed from 6401px to 5436px |
| **Buyer-Resonance Filtering** | All features shown → only buyer-relevant features | LoneScale rebuilt around "the part that resonates most with real buyers" |

The universal pattern: before pages are **too wide** (too many use cases, buyers, features). After pages are **narrower but deeper** (one buyer, one problem, one category -- proved thoroughly).

### The Homepage Positioning Rubric

Score any B2B homepage on 7 dimensions (1 = weak, 3 = strong):

| Dimension | What It Measures |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Problem Clarity** | Does the page name a specific, buyer-owned problem? |
| **Category Anchor** | Can the visitor place this product in a recognizable category? |
| **Product Explanation** | Does the visitor understand what the product actually does? |
| **Differentiation** | Does the page explain why this product vs. alternatives? |
| **Point of View** | Does the company take an opinionated stance on the market? |
| **Proof & Credibility** | Are there specific metrics, named customers, or concrete results? |
| **Page Architecture** | Does the page follow a clear hero → problem → solution → proof → CTA flow? |

**Scoring:** 18-21 = strong (market-ready), 13-17 = functional (has gaps), 7-12 = needs repositioning. See [[concepts/homepage-positioning]] for the full rubric with weak/adequate/strong criteria per dimension.

## Examples from Client Work

### Strongest Homepage Mapping: Avoca

Avoca's framework translates cleanly to a homepage:
- **Hero:** "Your growth is hitting a ceiling" + subhead about missed calls and revenue leakage
- **Problem:** Three pain points (underfilled boards, inconsistent CX, staffing challenges) with vivid data ($46K per churn event, 90% of leads gone in 20 seconds)
- **Solution:** Avoca's agentic platform for the AI front office
- **Feature sections:** "Book more jobs" / "Deliver exceptional experiences" / "Scale your front office" -- each with specific USPs
- **Proof:** Named customers (My Plumber Plus, Aire Serv), hard metrics (13% revenue growth, $800K campaign, 75% booking rate)
- **CTA:** Demo or pilot

Every section draws directly from the framework. The proof section is unusually strong because Avoca has named customers with quantified results.

### Homepage Structural Insight: Unity Games

Unity Games illustrates how a multi-persona framework maps to a single homepage. The "develop, deploy, grow" pillar structure provides three feature sections, but the homepage must choose a primary audience. Developers are the primary buyer -- the hero section should speak to them. UA and monetization personas get served through the "deploy" and "grow" sections without diluting the developer-first framing.

### Homepage Without Proof: Agency

Agency's framework would produce a strong homepage through the feature sections (bold value pillars: "Single point of contact," "Complete context on every customer," "Infinite capacity to serve") but would struggle at the proof section. No named customers or hard metrics means the proof section relies on capability claims and vision -- weaker than Avoca's data-rich proof.

### Before/After Insight: CommandBar

CommandBar's repositioning illustrates the most common homepage fix: category anchoring. Before, the page listed use cases without a frame -- the visitor couldn't answer "what kind of tool is this?" After, the page anchored to "digital adoption tools" and added a strong POV. The visitor could immediately understand the product category and the company's stance on it.

### Before/After Insight: Concierge AI

Concierge AI's repositioning illustrates the page architecture problem. The before page was ~550px -- essentially just a hero with no supporting structure. The after page expanded to ~3076px with full positioning architecture: problem framing, solution introduction, feature sections, and proof. The lesson: a homepage that's too thin fails not because it lacks content but because it lacks *argument*.

## Patterns & Lessons

- **The homepage is primary-buyer only.** The homepage should speak to the primary buyer. Secondary buyers get their own landing pages. Trying to address multiple buyers on the homepage is the most common positioning failure (see [[concepts/two-buyer-problem]]).
- **Feature sections should be organized around value pillars, not product modules.** "Book more jobs" converts better than "AI Call Handling" because the buyer cares about outcomes, not mechanisms.
- **The three-question test is universal.** Every weak homepage fails at least one of: What problem? What do you do? Why you? Every strong homepage answers all three above the fold.
- **Category anchoring is the most underrated move.** Startups resist being "put in a box," but buyers need a box to evaluate. The before/after corpus consistently shows category anchoring as a primary fix.
- **Opinionated beats descriptive.** The single biggest upgrade in the homepage corpus is adding a point of view. Description tells the buyer *what*; opinion tells them *why it matters*.
- **Page depth should match positioning complexity.** Some pages need to expand (Concierge AI: too thin, no argument). Others need to compress (Omnibound: too sprawling, no focus). Right-size the page to the positioning architecture.
- **Proof gaps are homepage killers.** The gap between a strong narrative (hero + problem + features) and weak proof (no logos, no metrics, no quotes) is where visitors bounce. Only 3 of 11 Strata clients have proof sections strong enough for a high-converting homepage.
