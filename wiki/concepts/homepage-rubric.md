# Homepage Rubric

## Definition

A scoring rubric for evaluating the quality of a B2B SaaS homepage across six dimensions. This rubric produces a diagnostic that identifies exactly where a homepage is strong, where it breaks, and what to fix first.

## What This Rubric Is For

This rubric evaluates **homepages** -- the most compressed expression of a company's full positioning. A homepage has one job: to make the right buyer immediately understand what the company does, who it's for, what problem it solves, and why they should care -- before they scroll, before they click, before they think about leaving.

A homepage is not a messaging framework. It is not a POV narrative. It is graded on compression, clarity, and narrative architecture -- not structural completeness or persuasive arc. Each document type has different evaluation criteria because each serves a different function:

- A **homepage** is a compressed positioning artifact. It must accomplish in a single scroll what a framework does across 20+ pages. It is graded on hero clarity, audience consistency, problem framing, value communication, competitive differentiation, and proof placement.
- A **messaging framework** is a structured reference architecture. It is graded on structural completeness, pillar construction, buyer specificity, and competitive clarity -- criteria that are irrelevant for a homepage.
- A **POV narrative** is a persuasive story. It is graded on opening hook, arc integrity, and emotional momentum -- criteria that don't apply to a homepage.
- A **pitch deck** is a narrative delivery format. It is graded on slide-level clarity, narrative flow across slides, and proof slide structure.

The best homepages feel inevitable -- every section earns the next, every claim is specific, and the right buyer finishes scrolling knowing exactly why this company exists and why the alternatives are wrong.

## Positioning Stance -- Read This First

Every review must account for which positioning stance the asset is pursuing. The stance dictates what "good" looks like -- the same dimension can demand opposite execution depending on the stance. Before evaluating any dimension, determine which stance applies.

### Stance 1: Creating a New Category

- **Reference point:** a job-to-be-done of your choosing
- **Competitive alternative:** the status quo way people do that JTBD (NOT named vendors)
- **Target customer:** people doing the JTBD you selected, who don't know a category exists
- **What good looks like:** indicts the status quo approach/architecture, not competitors. Names a new category. Educates the buyer that the problem is real and unsolved. Invokes an inevitable future. Longest game, highest risk, highest reward.
- **Stance error to flag:** naming or comparing against competitor brands. A category creator who benchmarks against named vendors is undermining their own category -- they're implicitly admitting they're in someone else's category.

### Stance 2: Competing in a Mature Category

- **Reference point:** the category name itself (the category already exists in buyers' minds)
- **Competitive alternative:** other named vendors in the category
- **Target customer:** people already shopping the category
- **What good looks like:** sharp, specific differentiation against named competitors. Clear reason-to-choose-us within an understood frame. Mind space and budget already exist -- the job is to win the comparison, not create the need.
- **Stance error to flag:** failing to differentiate against named competitors, or trying to educate the buyer on a problem they already understand. In a mature category, spending the asset explaining why the problem matters is wasted -- the buyer knows. Vague "we're better" claims without specific head-to-head contrast also fail.

### Stance 3: Competing in an Immature Category

- **Reference point:** the job-to-be-done of the category
- **Competitive alternative:** the status quo way people do that JTBD
- **Target customer:** people doing the JTBD who may not know the category exists yet
- **What good looks like:** balances light category education with differentiation. Positions to become a leader as the category solidifies. Some teaching required, but not from scratch. Indicts the status quo while gesturing at the emerging category.
- **Stance error to flag:** positioning as if the category is mature (over-indexing on competitor comparison when buyers don't yet know the category) OR positioning as if creating from scratch (over-educating when an emerging category already has some awareness). The immature stance requires calibration between the two.

## How Stance Changes This Evaluation

The positioning stance shifts what each dimension should look for:

- **Hero Clarity:** For a category creator, the hero must name the new category and make it immediately intelligible to a cold visitor who has never heard of it -- clarity is paramount because the category itself is new. For a mature-category player, the hero can use the existing category name as shorthand and focus the tagline on differentiation within the known frame. For an immature-category player, the hero must balance category naming with problem framing -- the visitor may have heard of the emerging category but doesn't fully understand it yet.

- **Audience Mapping:** Stance does not change this dimension's bar. The page must speak consistently to the primary buyer regardless of stance.

- **Problem Clarity:** This dimension carries more weight for a category creator -- they must establish the problem because the buyer doesn't know it exists. The homepage needs to spend real estate making the buyer feel a problem they haven't yet named for themselves. For a mature-category player, problem clarity carries less weight -- the buyer already feels the problem; belaboring it wastes the fold. The homepage should acknowledge the problem quickly and move to differentiation. For an immature-category player, problem clarity requires a middle path -- enough framing to educate, not so much that it feels like a lecture.

- **Value Clarity:** For a category creator, value pillars should name outcomes that are impossible under the status quo approach -- they must make the buyer see what they couldn't get before. For a mature-category player, value pillars should name outcomes where this company specifically outperforms named alternatives. For an immature-category player, value pillars should balance category-level outcomes with company-specific advantages.

- **Competitive Point of View:** For a category creator, the competitive POV indicts the status quo approach as architecturally broken -- not named competitors. The bright line is drawn between the old way (JTBD approach) and the new way (the category being created). For a mature-category player, the competitive POV must implicitly or explicitly reposition named alternatives -- the buyer is comparing vendors, so the page must win that comparison. For an immature-category player, the competitive POV indicts the status quo while also signaling why this company will lead the emerging category over early entrants.

- **Proof & Social Validation:** For a category creator, proof must validate that the new category works at all -- early adopter stories and outcome metrics that demonstrate the category's viability. For a mature-category player, proof must validate competitive superiority -- head-to-head wins, migration stories, and metrics that show this vendor outperforms alternatives. For an immature-category player, proof should demonstrate both category viability and company leadership within the emerging space.

## Stance-Execution Mismatch

Before scoring, determine whether the asset's actual execution matches the stance the user selected. A common and costly error is choosing one stance strategically but executing as if pursuing another. Examples:

- Selected "creating a category" but the asset is full of head-to-head competitor comparisons (executing as mature-category)
- Selected "mature category" but the asset spends most of its energy educating the buyer on a problem they already understand (executing as category creation)
- Selected "immature category" but positions with no category education at all (executing as mature) or pure greenfield education (executing as creation)

When you detect a mismatch, this is one of the most valuable findings you can surface. Name it explicitly. Explain what stance the execution actually reflects versus what they selected. Then guide them to commit to their chosen direction -- help them stay the course. The goal is not to tell them which stance is right; it's to help them execute consistently with the strategic direction they've chosen. A confused stance produces confused positioning.

## The Six Dimensions

### 1. Hero Clarity

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does the tagline pass the cold visitor test?** A stranger with no context should understand what the company does and who it's for within 5 seconds. If you covered the logo and read only the hero, could you identify the company's category, buyer, and primary value? If not, the hero fails.
- **Is the tagline clear before it's clever?** Clever at the expense of clear fails. The tagline's job is recognition ("this is for me"), not intrigue ("that's interesting").
- **Does the subtext reinforce and sharpen the tagline -- or repeat it?** The subtext should add a specific dimension the tagline compressed away. "The AI platform for legal teams" as a tagline followed by "We help legal teams use AI to work faster" as subtext is repetition, not sharpening.
- **Is there any jargon, abstraction, or category language that requires insider knowledge to decode?** "Agentic workflow orchestration" means nothing to a cold visitor. "Your AI front office answers every call" does.
- **Would the target buyer read the hero section and immediately think "this is for me" -- or would they need to scroll to find relevance?** The hero must self-select the right buyer in seconds. If the buyer has to scroll to determine relevance, the hero has already failed.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Tagline is immediately clear to a cold visitor. Subtext sharpens not repeats. No jargon. Buyer thinks "this is for me" in under 5 seconds. Category, buyer, and value all identifiable from hero alone. |
| **4/5** | Clear and specific but subtext partially repeats tagline or takes 6-8 seconds to fully land. |
| **3/5** | Tagline is clever but requires context to decode. Category identifiable but buyer or value requires scrolling. |
| **2/5** | Tagline is abstract or jargon-heavy. Cold visitor cannot identify what the company does without scrolling. |
| **1/5** | Hero fails the cold visitor test entirely. Could belong to any company in any category. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** A home services AI platform (Series B) led with a tagline naming the buyer's felt pain -- a growth ceiling -- immediately followed by subtext that sharpened the claim around missed calls, revenue leakage, and front office bottlenecks. A cold visitor could identify the category (AI front office), the buyer (home services operator), and the value (stop losing revenue to missed calls) without scrolling. The tagline was clear before it was clever.
- **Weak (1-2):** A before-state homepage that addressed a real capability but had no clear owner for the problem. The hero described a real product but no specific buyer could read it and think "this is for me." The problem was diffuse, the product was unclear, and the category was absent. Covering the logo, you couldn't identify what kind of company it was.

### 2. Audience Mapping

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does every section below the hero speak consistently to the same buyer the hero addressed?** The hero targets a specific role. Every section below it should feel like it was written for that same person.
- **Are there sections that drift to a different persona -- a secondary buyer, a different use case, a different stage?** The hero targets the VP of Engineering but a case study is from a CTO and a testimonial is from an IC -- this is audience drift.
- **Would the primary buyer feel the entire page was written for them -- or would they hit a section that feels irrelevant?** The acid test: if the primary buyer reads every section and one feels "not for me," the page has drifted.
- **Are the use cases, testimonials, and proof points all from the same buyer type -- or do they scatter across multiple personas?** Proof from the wrong buyer is worse than no proof at all -- it signals the page isn't sure who it's for.
- **Does the page maintain a single point of view throughout, or does it try to be all things to all buyers?** The homepage is primary-buyer only. Secondary buyers get their own landing pages.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Every section speaks to the same buyer. Use cases, proof, and testimonials all from the primary persona. Single consistent point of view. |
| **4/5** | Mostly consistent but one section drifts to a secondary buyer or use case. |
| **3/5** | Two or more sections feel written for a different buyer. Page tries to serve multiple personas with equal weight. |
| **2/5** | No clear primary buyer. Page reads as a category overview rather than a targeted message to a specific person. |
| **1/5** | Actively contradictory -- different sections make incompatible claims about who the product is for. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** A home services AI platform (Series B) spoke to the owner-operator throughout. The hero named their growth ceiling. The feature sections used their language -- framing value around booking jobs, scaling the front office, and call performance. The proof came from named home services businesses in the target segment. The metrics were in the buyer's terms (booking rate, revenue growth). No section drifted to a developer, a marketing VP, or a CTO. The owner-operator read the entire page and every section felt written for them.
- **Weak (2/5):** A before-state homepage that focused on high-level benefits rather than concrete use cases. The messaging was abstract enough that multiple buyer roles could claim it but none felt targeted. No single buyer read the page and felt it was written for them specifically.

### 3. Problem Clarity

**What to evaluate:**

- **Is the problem the company solves clearly felt somewhere on the page -- even if there's no dedicated problem section?** The problem doesn't need its own section, but it must be present and felt.
- **Does the problem appear near the top -- in the hero, the subtext, or the first section below the fold?** A solution without a felt problem has no emotional weight. The problem must land before the solution claims it resolves.
- **Is the problem named specifically -- or is it implied through the solution framing?** "Your team wastes 40% of their week on manual contract review" is specific. "We make legal teams more efficient" implies a problem but never names it.
- **Would a buyer who has this problem recognize it immediately -- or would they have to infer it from the solution?** Recognition is instant. Inference requires work. The best homepages name the problem so precisely the buyer thinks "how do they know my life?"
- **Is the problem framed as a structural issue (why it keeps happening) or just a symptom (what happens)?** "You're missing calls because your front office can't scale with demand" is structural. "You're missing calls" is a symptom. Structural framing creates urgency because it tells the buyer the problem won't fix itself.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Problem is clearly felt near the top of the page. Named specifically or powerfully implied. Buyer with this problem immediately recognizes it. Framed structurally not symptomatically. |
| **4/5** | Problem is clear but appears slightly below the fold or is implied rather than named. |
| **3/5** | Problem is present but vague -- buyer has to do interpretive work to connect it to their reality. |
| **2/5** | Problem is buried or absent. Page leads with solution without establishing why the solution matters. |
| **1/5** | No problem framing. Page is entirely solution and feature focused. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** A homepage repositioned from a generic product page to one that names the structural problem with existing tools in its category: they're annoying, they interrupt the user, they're built for the company's goals not the user's experience. The problem was felt immediately and framed as an architectural flaw in the category, not just a symptom. The buyer (product manager) recognized it because they'd been embarrassed by their own product tours.
- **Weak (1-2):** A before-state homepage with a minimal hero (~550px) and no supporting structure. No problem framing at all. The page jumped straight to what the product was without establishing why the buyer should care. A visitor could understand the product category but had no reason to feel urgency.

### 4. Value Clarity

**What to evaluate:**

- **Are three distinct value ideas coming through on the page -- not features, not capabilities, but outcomes the buyer gets?** "Real-time analytics dashboard" is a feature. "Know exactly where revenue is leaking before it's gone" is value.
- **Does each value idea feel distinct -- or do they blur together into variations of the same claim?** Three pillars that all say "save time" in different words are one pillar repeated three times.
- **Is the value framed in the buyer's language (what they get) or the vendor's language (what the product does)?** "Book more jobs" is buyer language. "AI-powered call handling" is vendor language about the same capability.
- **Could a buyer articulate the three value ideas after scrolling the page once -- or are they buried in feature descriptions?** The value ideas must be scannable. If a buyer has to read paragraphs to extract the value, it's buried.
- **Does the value section connect back to the problem established earlier -- or does it feel disconnected?** The strongest homepages make each value idea feel like the direct resolution of a specific dimension of the problem.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Three distinct outcome-based value ideas. Buyer language throughout. Each value idea is memorable and distinct. Connects back to problem. |
| **4/5** | Three value ideas present but one is slightly feature-based or blurs with another. |
| **3/5** | Value ideas present but framed as features or capabilities. Buyer has to translate to outcomes. |
| **2/5** | No clear value structure. Features listed without outcome framing. |
| **1/5** | Entirely feature and capability focused. No buyer outcome language. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** A home services AI platform (Series B) built three value pillars around distinct buyer outcomes -- booking more jobs, delivering exceptional experiences, and scaling the front office. Each was in the owner-operator's language, scannable, memorable, and connected to a different dimension of the growth ceiling problem. A buyer scrolling the page once could repeat all three. None described what the product does -- they described what the buyer gets.
- **Weak (2/5):** A homepage that lists "AI-powered automation," "Real-time analytics," "Seamless integrations" as its three sections. These are features dressed as value. The buyer has to do the work of translating each feature into an outcome. After scrolling, they can recall what the product does but not what they get.

### 5. Competitive Point of View

**What to evaluate:**

- **Is it clear who and what this company is against -- without naming them?** The best homepages don't name competitors. They indict the approach, the model, the status quo.
- **Does the page signal a distinct point of view that implicitly repositions the alternatives as wrong or insufficient?** Not just "we're better" but "the way everyone else does this is architecturally broken."
- **Does the company sound like everyone else in the category -- or does it have a voice and perspective that's immediately distinguishable?** The logo-swap test: could you replace the logo with a competitor's and the page would still make sense? If yes, there's no competitive POV.
- **Is there a "bright line" moment on the page -- a claim or framing that makes the alternative approach look structurally incapable?** Not just worse. Incapable. The bright line draws a division that the buyer can't unsee.
- **Would a buyer who has been talking to competitors read this page and immediately understand why this company is different -- not just better?** Different is structural. Better is incremental. The homepage must communicate structural difference.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Distinct POV that implicitly repositions alternatives. Bright line moment present. Voice is immediately distinguishable from category. Competitor-aware buyer immediately understands the difference. |
| **4/5** | Distinct POV but bright line is implied rather than explicit. Voice is differentiated but one section sounds like category language. |
| **3/5** | Some differentiation but could easily be confused with a competitor page if the logo were swapped. No bright line moment. |
| **2/5** | Sounds like the category. Generic claims that any competitor could make. No implicit repositioning. |
| **1/5** | Indistinguishable from competitors. Could be any company in the space. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** A homepage that took a highly opinionated stance on its category of digital adoption tools. The page didn't just describe the product -- it indicted the entire category's approach as user-hostile. Existing tools were framed as annoying interruptions designed for the company, not the user. This created a bright line: tools built for the company vs. tools built for the user. A product manager who had evaluated three vendors read this and immediately understood why the company was structurally different, not just incrementally better.
- **Weak (1-2):** A before-state homepage that covered a variety of use cases without an anchoring element -- differentiation was not specified and the page lacked a strong POV. The page described capabilities without taking any position on the market. A competitor could have used the same page with their logo. No buyer would finish scrolling and understand why this company existed rather than any other tool.

### 6. Proof & Social Validation

**What to evaluate:**

- **Is there credible proof on the page -- customer quotes, logos, metrics, case study callouts?** The presence of proof matters, but quality matters more.
- **Does the proof reinforce the specific value claims made above it -- or does it feel like a generic logo wall disconnected from the narrative?** A logo wall with no context is a missed opportunity. Proof must be tied to the specific claims the page makes.
- **Are quotes specific and outcome-based -- or vague and generic?** A quote citing double-digit revenue growth in the first quarter after adoption passes. "Great platform, love using it!" fails. Specific quotes let the buyer picture themselves getting the same result.
- **Are metrics specific and traceable to a mechanism -- or are they unverifiable vanity numbers?** A metric tying a specific dollar amount to the product's booking or campaign mechanism passes. "10x faster" fails -- faster than what? measured how?
- **Is the proof placed correctly -- near the value claims it validates, not isolated in a separate "customers" section that breaks the narrative?** Proof adjacent to claims creates reinforcement. Proof isolated in a separate section creates a gap the buyer has to bridge.
- **Is the proof from the right buyer type -- or does it come from a different persona than the one the page is targeting?** A homepage targeting owner-operators with a testimonial from an enterprise CTO has a persona mismatch. The proof must come from the buyer the page is written for.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Specific, outcome-based proof. Placed adjacent to the claims it validates. From the right buyer type. Metrics are specific and mechanistic. |
| **4/5** | Strong proof but placement is slightly disconnected from the claims it validates, or one quote is slightly generic. |
| **3/5** | Proof is present but generic -- logo wall without context, vague quotes, or unverifiable metrics. |
| **2/5** | Minimal proof. One or two logos with no quotes or metrics. |
| **1/5** | No proof. Or proof is from the wrong buyer type entirely. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** A home services AI platform (Series B) featured named customers from the target segment, hard metrics tied to specific outcomes (double-digit revenue growth, significant campaign-attributed revenue, high booking rates), all from home services businesses the prospect recognized as peers. The metrics were traceable to the product's mechanism (booking rate connects to the "book more jobs" pillar, revenue growth connects to the growth ceiling problem). The proof validated the specific claims made in the feature sections above it.
- **Weak (2-3):** A homepage with bold value pillars around single-point-of-contact service, complete customer context, and infinite capacity -- but no named customers and no hard metrics. The proof section relied on capability claims and vision, which means the page made promises it couldn't prove. The buyer was asked to believe, not shown evidence.

## What a Perfect Homepage Looks Like

A 5/5 across all six dimensions means:

1. **Cold visitor understands category, buyer, and value from the hero alone in under 5 seconds.** The tagline is clear before it's clever. The subtext sharpens, not repeats.
2. **Every section speaks to the same buyer -- no drift, no persona confusion.** The primary buyer reads the entire page and every section feels written for them.
3. **Problem is clearly felt near the top, framed structurally not symptomatically.** The buyer recognizes their problem before the solution is introduced.
4. **Three distinct outcome-based value ideas in buyer language, connected back to the problem.** Each value idea is scannable, memorable, and distinct from the others.
5. **Distinct POV with a bright line moment -- competitor-aware buyer immediately understands the difference.** The page doesn't just describe the product. It takes a position on the market.
6. **Specific, outcome-based proof placed adjacent to the claims it validates.** Proof from the right buyer type with metrics tied to the product's mechanism.

**The total score range is 6-30.** Use this guide:

| Score | Assessment |
|-------|------------|
| **27-30** | Production-ready. Homepage can drive paid traffic and outbound without revision. |
| **22-26** | Strong foundation. One or two dimensions need tightening. |
| **16-21** | Structural gaps. Hero or value clarity is weak. Needs a revision pass before scaling traffic. |
| **10-15** | Needs rework. Multiple dimensions weak. Driving traffic to this page will produce poor conversion. |
| **6-9** | Start over. Homepage describes the category not the company. |

## Common Homepage Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Clever Hero That Fails the Cold Visitor Test

The tagline is interesting but requires context to decode. A cold visitor reads it and thinks "intriguing" rather than "this is for me." Clever is fine -- but only after clear. If covering the logo makes the company unidentifiable, the hero has failed.

### Mistake 2: Subtext That Repeats the Tagline

The subtext should add specificity the tagline compressed away -- not restate the tagline in different words. "The AI platform for legal teams" as a tagline followed by "We help legal teams use AI to work faster" as subtext is repetition, not sharpening. The subtext must earn its space by adding a dimension the tagline left out -- the specific mechanism, the specific buyer, or the specific problem.

### Mistake 3: Problem Buried Below the Fold

Leading with the solution before establishing why it matters. The buyer hasn't felt the problem yet, so the solution has no emotional weight. The problem doesn't need its own section -- but it must be felt somewhere near the top. One before-state engagement had ~550px of hero with no problem framing at all. The after version expanded to ~3076px -- not because more is better, but because the page needed room to build an argument.

### Mistake 4: Feature Sections Dressed as Value

"Real-time analytics dashboard" is a feature. "Know exactly where revenue is leaking before it's gone" is value. The strongest homepages never describe what the product does -- they describe what the buyer gets. Outcome-oriented value pillar headlines (like "Real-time visibility" or "Automated workflows") convert better than product module names ("Analytics Dashboard," "Workflow Engine") because the buyer cares about outcomes, not architecture.

### Mistake 5: Generic Social Proof

A logo wall with no context. Vague quotes that could describe any product ("We love using [Product]!"). Metrics without a mechanism ("10x faster" -- faster than what? how?). Proof must be specific enough that the buyer can picture themselves getting the same result. One engagement illustrates the risk: strong value pillars but no named customers or hard metrics meant the proof section asked the buyer to believe rather than showing them evidence.

### Mistake 6: Sounding Like the Category

Every claim on the page could appear on a competitor's page without modification. No distinct point of view. No implicit repositioning. One before-state engagement is the archetype: the page covered a variety of use cases without an anchoring element, differentiation was not specified, and the page lacked a strong POV. The page described capabilities without explaining why the company sees the market differently. The buyer who had been talking to three competitors read this page and learned nothing new.

### Mistake 7: Audience Drift

The hero targets one buyer. The case studies are from a different buyer. The testimonials are from a third. The CTA is written for a fourth. No single person reads this page and feels it was written entirely for them. One before-state engagement illustrates a subtler version: the problem was real but had no clear owner, so the page couldn't maintain audience consistency because it never established an audience in the first place. The fix was to refocus around a clear use case and a clear owner -- which gave every subsequent section a consistent audience to address.
