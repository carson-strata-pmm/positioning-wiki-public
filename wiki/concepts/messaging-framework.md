# Messaging Framework

## Definition

A messaging framework is the single operational document that captures a company's entire go-to-market story in a structured, usable format. It is not a brand book, a pitch deck, or a marketing brief -- it is the source of truth that every team draws from when speaking to the market. The framework contains everything from the buyer definition through the copy bank, organized so that marketing can pull headlines, sales can build talk tracks, and product can align roadmap language -- all from the same document.

## How It Works

### What a Messaging Framework Contains

Every Strata messaging framework follows the same structural spine, though depth varies by engagement complexity:

| Section | What It Contains | Who Uses It |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Audience / ICP** | Primary and secondary buyer personas, trigger events, firmographic and psychographic signals | Marketing (targeting), Sales (qualification), Product (prioritization) |
| **Hero Problem** | The named systemic challenge -- 2-4 words -- with root causes and consequence cascade | Marketing (hero copy), Sales (discovery questions), Exec (board narrative) |
| **Pain Pillars** | Three sub-problems, each with pain statement, causes, and consequences | Marketing (content topics), Sales (pain discovery), CS (churn signals) |
| **Solution Introduction** | How the product is named and positioned within a category | Marketing (positioning line), Sales (elevator pitch), Product (category strategy) |
| **Value Pillars** | Three value statements, each inverting a pain pillar | Marketing (feature section headlines), Sales (demo structure), Product (roadmap themes) |
| **USPs** | 2-4 unique selling points per value pillar | Marketing (feature pages), Sales (competitive objection handling) |
| **Outcomes** | Measurable business results per pillar | Marketing (proof sections), Sales (ROI conversations), CS (success metrics) |
| **Differentiation** | Structured comparison vs. status quo, competitors, and adjacent categories | Marketing (comparison pages), Sales (competitive battle cards) |
| **Copy Bank** | Taglines, 25-word, 50-word, elevator pitch, positioning statement | Marketing (website, ads, email), Sales (outbound sequences), PR (press materials) |
| **Proof Points** | Metrics, logos, customer quotes, industry statistics | Marketing (case studies), Sales (trust building), Exec (investor narrative) |

### What a Messaging Framework Is Not

| Framework | NOT a Framework |
|-----------|----------------|
| Operational blueprint for GTM execution | A creative brief for a single campaign |
| Structured, searchable, reusable | A narrative meant to be read end-to-end |
| Governs all customer-facing language | Governs internal culture or values |
| Updated as positioning evolves | Written once and archived |

The POV narrative and the messaging framework are distinct documents that serve different audiences. The POV is an internal alignment story -- it tells the company *why* the narrative matters. The framework is the operational output -- it tells teams *what to say*. See [[concepts/point-of-view]] for the full POV/framework distinction.

### How Strata Builds a Messaging Framework

The framework is constructed top-down. Each layer depends on the one above it:

```
Discovery (interviews, competitive analysis, existing assets)
    ↓
ICP definition (who are we talking to?)
    ↓
Hero problem (what's their systemic challenge?)
    ↓
Pain pillars (how does the problem decompose?)
    ↓
Solution introduction (what do we call the product?)
    ↓
Value pillars (what does the buyer get?)
    ↓
USPs + Outcomes (what proves it?)
    ↓
Differentiation (why us vs. alternatives?)
    ↓
Copy bank (ready-to-use language)
    ↓
Proof points (evidence that makes it credible)
```

**The top-down dependency is critical.** If the hero problem changes, the pillars must change. If the ICP shifts, the hero problem may shift. Frameworks that are built bottom-up -- starting with features and working up to a narrative -- produce messaging that feels assembled rather than argued.

### How Different Teams Use the Framework

**Marketing** uses the framework as a content engine. The hero problem feeds the homepage hero. Value pillars become feature section headlines. The copy bank provides ready-to-paste language for ads, emails, and landing pages. Proof points feed case studies and social proof sections.

**Sales** uses the framework as a conversation structure. The hero problem opens discovery calls. Pain pillars become qualifying questions. Value pillars structure the demo. Differentiation feeds objection handling. The copy bank provides outbound email language.

**Product** uses the framework as a strategic alignment tool. Value pillars map to roadmap themes. USPs define what must be demonstrable in the product. The ICP section clarifies which buyer's pain to prioritize.

**Executive team** uses the framework as the investor and board narrative. The hero problem anchors the market opportunity slide. The category positioning defines the competitive moat. Proof points validate traction.

## Examples from Client Work

### Strongest Framework: Avoca

Avoca's framework is the most operationally complete in the corpus. Every section is populated with specific, usable content:
- **ICP** has three buyer tiers with distinct pain language for each
- **Pain pillars** have full cause-consequence stacks with quantified data ($46K per churn event, 90% of leads gone in 20 seconds)
- **Value pillars** are pure aspiration ("Book more jobs," "Scale your front office") -- no feature language
- **USPs** are verifiable in a demo (per-call ROI tracking, <3 second answer time)
- **Copy bank** includes multiple formats ready for deployment
- **Proof points** include named customers with hard metrics ($800K single-branch campaign, 13% revenue growth)

The Avoca framework can be handed to a marketer, a sales rep, or a content writer and immediately produce on-brand, on-strategy output.

### Strongest Framework: TeamOhana

TeamOhana's framework has the tightest structural alignment. Every pain pillar maps cleanly to a value pillar maps cleanly to an outcome. The copy bank is extensive -- 6+ elevator pitch alternatives show the iteration process, which is itself instructive. The draft framework preserves scratch space with alternative pillar structures and naming explorations, making it a useful reference for how frameworks evolve.

### Most Complex Framework: Eudia

Eudia required five documents: two POV narratives, two messaging frameworks, and a messaging map explaining how they relate. The CLO framework and ALF framework share a hero problem space but have completely different pillar structures, buyer language, and proof points. The Messaging Map is a unique meta-deliverable that explains document relationships -- a useful model for any engagement with multiple audiences.

### Weakest Framework: Snappy

Snappy's framework illustrates common weaknesses:
- **Pain pillars** are vague -- Pillar 1 restates the hero problem rather than decomposing it
- **Value pillars** are single-word abstractions ("Expertise," "Scale," "Delight") that lack specificity
- **Proof points** are third-party research stats with no named customers
- **Consequences** are unquantified ("gifts don't land")

The framework describes the product space but doesn't build a persuasive argument.

## Patterns & Lessons

- **The framework is only as strong as its weakest layer.** A brilliant hero problem with vague value pillars produces messaging that opens strong and fades. A strong copy bank built on weak differentiation produces language that sounds good but doesn't convince.
- **Symmetry is the quality test.** If every pain pillar maps to a value pillar maps to an outcome, the framework is structurally sound. If the mapping breaks -- a value pillar with no corresponding pain, an outcome with no supporting USP -- the messaging has a hole.
- **Copy banks are undervalued.** Across engagements, the copy bank (taglines, 25-word, 50-word, elevator pitch) is often the most-used output. It gets pasted directly into websites, pitch decks, outbound sequences, and sales scripts.
- **Frameworks should be updated, not rewritten.** As the product evolves, the framework should evolve with it. The hero problem and category positioning are durable; USPs and proof points change as the product ships new features and closes new customers.
- **One framework per audience, not one framework for all.** Eudia's dual framework is the exception that proves the rule. Most multi-buyer engagements can use one framework with persona-specific sections. But when buyers have fundamentally different motivations (CLO vs. law firm partner), separate frameworks are needed.
- **The framework predicts downstream quality.** The engagements with the strongest frameworks (Avoca, TeamOhana, Agency) also have the most usable copy banks, the clearest homepage direction, and the most coherent sales narratives. Weak frameworks cascade into weak everything.
