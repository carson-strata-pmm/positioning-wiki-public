# POV Narrative Rubric

## Definition

A scoring rubric for evaluating the quality of a B2B SaaS POV narrative across five dimensions. This rubric produces a diagnostic that identifies exactly where a narrative is strong, where it breaks, and what to fix first.

## What This Rubric Is For

This rubric evaluates **POV narratives** -- the persuasive story that makes the reader feel the problem so acutely that the company's introduction feels inevitable, not like a pitch. It is graded on narrative criteria only. Do not evaluate a POV on structural criteria that belong to a messaging framework (pillar balance, ICP specificity, USP uniqueness). Each document type has different evaluation criteria because each serves a different function:

- A **POV narrative** is a persuasive story. It has a job: to build tension so precisely that the company's introduction feels like the only possible resolution. It is graded on opening hook, arc integrity, resolution quality, writing flow, and overall narrative strength.
- A **messaging framework** is a structured reference architecture. It is graded on structural completeness, pillar construction, buyer specificity, and competitive clarity -- criteria that are irrelevant for a narrative.
- A **homepage** is a compressed expression of the framework. It is graded on above-the-fold clarity, page architecture, and CTA design.
- A **pitch deck** is a narrative delivery format. It is graded on slide-level clarity, narrative flow across slides, and proof slide structure.

Evaluating a POV on structural criteria (e.g., "the pillars aren't balanced") or evaluating a framework on narrative criteria (e.g., "the opening isn't compelling") produces meaningless feedback. Match the rubric to the document type.

## Positioning Stance -- Read This First

Every review must account for which positioning stance the asset is pursuing. The stance dictates what "good" looks like -- the same dimension can demand opposite execution depending on the stance. Before evaluating any dimension, determine which stance applies.

### Stance 1: Creating a New Category

- **Reference point:** a job-to-be-done of your choosing
- **Competitive alternative:** the status quo way people do that JTBD (NOT named vendors)
- **Target customer:** people doing the JTBD you selected, who don't know a category exists
- **What good looks like:** indicts the status quo approach/architecture, not competitors. Names a new category. Educates the buyer that the problem is real and unsolved. Invokes an inevitable future. Longest game, highest risk, highest reward.
- **Stance error to flag:** naming or comparing against competitor brands. A category creator who benchmarks against named vendors is undermining their own category -- they're implicitly admitting they're in someone else's category.

### Stance 2: Competing in a Mature Category

- **Reference point:** the category name itself (the category already exists in buyers' minds)
- **Competitive alternative:** other named vendors in the category
- **Target customer:** people already shopping the category
- **What good looks like:** sharp, specific differentiation against named competitors. Clear reason-to-choose-us within an understood frame. Mind space and budget already exist -- the job is to win the comparison, not create the need.
- **Stance error to flag:** failing to differentiate against named competitors, or trying to educate the buyer on a problem they already understand. In a mature category, spending the asset explaining why the problem matters is wasted -- the buyer knows. Vague "we're better" claims without specific head-to-head contrast also fail.

### Stance 3: Competing in an Immature Category

- **Reference point:** the job-to-be-done of the category
- **Competitive alternative:** the status quo way people do that JTBD
- **Target customer:** people doing the JTBD who may not know the category exists yet
- **What good looks like:** balances light category education with differentiation. Positions to become a leader as the category solidifies. Some teaching required, but not from scratch. Indicts the status quo while gesturing at the emerging category.
- **Stance error to flag:** positioning as if the category is mature (over-indexing on competitor comparison when buyers don't yet know the category) OR positioning as if creating from scratch (over-educating when an emerging category already has some awareness). The immature stance requires calibration between the two.

## How Stance Changes This Evaluation

The positioning stance shifts what each dimension should look for:

- **Opening Hook:** For a category creator, the opening must immerse the buyer in the status quo JTBD pain -- the buyer may not yet know a better way exists, so the opening earns its length by making the current approach feel absurd or broken. For a mature-category player, the opening can assume the buyer already feels the problem and move faster to differentiation or a provocative reframe. For an immature-category player, the opening should make the buyer feel the JTBD pain while hinting that an emerging solution category exists.

- **Arc Integrity:** The "Why Now" and "Failed Tries" beats are essential for category creators -- "Why Now" establishes why the world has changed enough to make a new category possible, and "Failed Tries" indicts the status quo approaches (not named vendors) as architecturally incapable. For mature-category players, these beats can be lighter because the buyer already accepts the category premise -- the arc can move faster to the pivot. For immature-category players, "Why Now" should name the shift that is creating the emerging category, and "Failed Tries" should indict both the status quo and the limitations of early category entrants.

- **Resolution & Vision:** For a category creator, the closing vision must paint the inevitable future the new category enables -- it's a world-order argument, not just a product outcome. For a mature-category player, the resolution should focus on specific competitive advantages and the closing vision can be more grounded in near-term buyer outcomes. For an immature-category player, the vision should position the company as the leader of a category that is about to arrive.

- **Writing Quality & Flow:** Stance does not change this dimension's bar. The writing must be tight regardless of stance.

- **Overall Narrative Strength:** For a category creator, narrative strength is measured by whether the reader finishes believing a new category must exist. For a mature-category player, it's measured by whether the reader finishes believing this company wins the comparison. For an immature-category player, it's measured by whether the reader finishes believing this company will lead the emerging category.

## Stance-Execution Mismatch

Before scoring, determine whether the asset's actual execution matches the stance the user selected. A common and costly error is choosing one stance strategically but executing as if pursuing another. Examples:

- Selected "creating a category" but the asset is full of head-to-head competitor comparisons (executing as mature-category)
- Selected "mature category" but the asset spends most of its energy educating the buyer on a problem they already understand (executing as category creation)
- Selected "immature category" but positions with no category education at all (executing as mature) or pure greenfield education (executing as creation)

When you detect a mismatch, this is one of the most valuable findings you can surface. Name it explicitly. Explain what stance the execution actually reflects versus what they selected. Then guide them to commit to their chosen direction -- help them stay the course. The goal is not to tell them which stance is right; it's to help them execute consistently with the strategic direction they've chosen. A confused stance produces confused positioning.

## The Full POV Arc

A POV narrative has a precise arc that must be followed in sequence. Each beat earns the next. Skipping or reordering beats produces a narrative that feels either rushed or meandering.

### Beat 1 -- Why Now

Something has changed in the world that makes this problem urgent today. Not "this has always been a problem" but "something shifted and now the stakes are higher." The reader should feel temporal pressure -- this is a moment, not a background condition.

### Beat 2 -- Rising Tension

The day-to-day reality of the buyer's world. The absurdity of how things currently work. The reader nods along -- "yes, this is exactly what it feels like." Visceral, specific, real. This beat earns its length by making the reader feel seen.

### Beat 3 -- Failed Tries

The alternatives the buyer has already attempted. Why each one fell short. Not because they're bad products but because they're architecturally incapable of solving the real problem. This is the most commonly skipped beat -- and without it, the pivot feels like a product pitch rather than an inevitable conclusion.

### Beat 4 -- The Issue

The paradox, tradeoff, or structural tension at the heart of the problem. The thing that makes this hard. The reason all the failed tries failed. This beat names the mechanism of failure -- not the failure itself, but the underlying architecture that guarantees failure.

### Beat 5 -- The Problem + Consequence

The named hero problem lands as the inevitable conclusion of everything above. Followed immediately by the consequence -- what happens if this goes unsolved. The hero problem should feel like a discovery the reader makes alongside the writer, not a thesis statement announced upfront.

### Beat 6 -- The Pivot

"This hasn't been solved. Until now." The single sentence or moment that turns the narrative from diagnosis to resolution. Must feel earned -- not like a product pitch. If the reader doesn't viscerally feel the problem by this point, the pivot fails regardless of how well it's written.

### Beat 7 -- The Introduction

The company is introduced as the inevitable answer to the problem the reader now fully feels. This beat has three sub-moves:

1. **Mechanism:** How it solves the problem -- not features, but the architectural reason this approach works where others couldn't.
2. **The Other Side:** What life looks like when the problem is gone. Specific, felt, in the buyer's language.
3. **Closing Vision:** The new world order this company is creating. Extends beyond the product to how the entire category or industry operates differently.

## The Five Dimensions

### 1. Opening Hook

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does the opening drop the reader directly into their world -- their day, their problem, their reality -- in the first sentence?** Not the market's reality. Not the industry's reality. The buyer's reality.
- **Is there any throat-clearing?** Context-setting, company history, market statistics that don't feel personal. Throat-clearing fails. The first sentence must earn the second.
- **Would the target buyer read the first sentence and feel seen -- or feel like they're reading a white paper?** "Seen" means the sentence describes something they personally experience. "White paper" means the sentence describes something they intellectually know.
- **Is the opening specific enough to feel real?** Generic openings ("In today's fast-paced business environment...") fail immediately. The opening should name a moment, a scenario, or a tension the buyer recognizes from their own experience.
- **Does the opening create forward momentum -- does it make the reader want to read the next sentence?** Momentum comes from tension, not information. A surprising claim, an uncomfortable truth, or a vivid scene creates momentum. A statistic or definition does not.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | First sentence drops the reader into their specific reality. No throat-clearing. Immediately creates forward momentum. Buyer feels seen. |
| **4/5** | Strong opening but takes 2-3 sentences to fully land. Minor context-setting before immersion. |
| **3/5** | Opening is relevant but starts at market level rather than buyer reality level. Reader is interested but not hooked. |
| **2/5** | Opening starts with company, product, or generic market observation. Reader has to work to find relevance. |
| **1/5** | Throat-clearing. Starts with history, statistics, or mission statement. No immersion. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** A POV that opens mid-scene in the buyer's specific operational reality -- a moment they recognize from their own day. The reader thinks "this is about me" before they've finished the first paragraph. (A legal AI platform (Series B) opened its CLO-targeted POV with the GC's daily reality of impossible tradeoffs, not with a market size statistic.)
- **Weak (1/2):** Opens with a market size statistic or a statement about industry trends. Accurate but impersonal. The reader is informed, not hooked. "The global legal market is worth $1 trillion" is true and irrelevant to the reader's felt experience.

### 2. Arc Integrity

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does the POV hit all seven beats in the correct sequence?** The sequence is non-negotiable: Why Now -> Rising Tension -> Failed Tries -> The Issue -> Problem + Consequence -> Pivot -> Introduction. Reordering weakens the argument.
- **Does each beat earn the next -- is there a logical cause-and-effect chain connecting them?** The reader should never have to ask "why are we talking about this now?" Each beat should feel like the only thing that could come next.
- **Are Failed Tries present?** This is the most commonly skipped beat. Without it, the pivot feels like a product pitch rather than an inevitable conclusion. Failed Tries are the proof that the problem isn't just unsolved but unsolvable by existing approaches.
- **Does the named hero problem land at Beat 5 -- after tension has been fully built -- or does it appear too early?** Naming the hero problem before the reader has felt the full weight of the problem robs it of its impact.
- **Is the pivot (Beat 6) a single clean moment, or does it get buried in explanation?** The pivot is a gear shift, not a paragraph. It should be felt as a turning point.
- **Does the company introduction (Beat 7) feel inevitable -- the only logical response to the problem -- or does it feel like a gear shift into sales mode?** If the reader feels a tonal shift from "insight" to "pitch" at Beat 7, the arc has failed.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | All seven beats present in sequence. Each beat earns the next. Failed Tries explicitly named. Hero problem lands after full tension build. Pivot is clean. Company introduction feels inevitable. |
| **4/5** | Six of seven beats present. One beat is thin or slightly out of sequence. Arc mostly holds. |
| **3/5** | Five beats present. Failed Tries or Consequence is missing. Hero problem named before tension is fully built. Arc has one significant gap. |
| **2/5** | Four or fewer beats. Major structural gaps. Narrative jumps from problem to solution without earning the pivot. |
| **1/5** | No discernible arc. Reads as a collection of true statements rather than a narrative with momentum. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** The narrative builds through rising tension, names three failed alternatives with specific reasons each fails, arrives at the hero problem as the only logical conclusion, pivots cleanly, and introduces the company as the inevitable answer. (A customer AI agent platform (Series A) built its Failed Tries section around specific alternative approaches, explaining the architectural reason each one breaks down at scale.)
- **Weak (2/5):** Opens with the problem, immediately introduces the company as the solution, closes with a vision. Accurate but unearned. The reader hasn't felt the problem deeply enough to believe the solution. No Failed Tries, no rising tension, no earned pivot.

### 3. Resolution & Vision

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does the back half deliver on the tension the front half built?** The resolution must feel proportional to the problem -- a big problem requires a big resolution. An eight-paragraph buildup followed by a two-sentence solution description creates an anticlimax.
- **Is the solution explanation mechanistic (how it actually works) or feature-based (what it does)?** Mechanism builds belief. Features build skepticism. "We use AI to automate X" is a feature. "Every interaction deepens a living understanding of your business so advice becomes more precise over time" is a mechanism.
- **Is "the other side" a specific felt reality or vague aspiration?** "Work better and move faster" fails. A specific description of what the buyer's day looks like when the problem is gone passes. The reader should be able to picture the scene.
- **Does the closing vision extend beyond the company to the world?** The strongest POVs close with a new world order -- a change in how the entire category or industry operates differently -- not just a product outcome. "Legal teams spend less time on review" is a product outcome. "Elite legal judgment becomes accessible to every company, not just those who can afford a top-ten firm" is a world-changing vision.
- **Is the closing vision connected to the opening tension?** The reader should feel the narrative has completed a full circle. The world described in the closing vision should be the direct resolution of the tension introduced in Beat 1.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Mechanism-based solution explanation. Specific "other side" in buyer's language. Closing vision extends to a new world order. Full circle back to opening tension. |
| **4/5** | Strong resolution but closing vision is slightly too product-focused or "other side" is slightly vague. |
| **3/5** | Solution is present but feature-based. "Other side" is aspirational but not specific. Closing vision is about the company not the world. |
| **2/5** | Solution section reads like a feature list. No "other side." Closing is a tagline rather than a vision. |
| **1/5** | No resolution. Narrative ends at the problem without delivering on the tension it built. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** The back half explains the specific mechanism by which the product eliminates the structural problem named in Beat 5, describes the buyer's day in concrete terms on the other side, and closes with a vision of how the entire category operates differently as a result. (An industrial 3D platform (enterprise) closed with a vision of how the entire manufacturing industry collaborates differently -- not just how one team uses the product.)
- **Weak (2/5):** "Introducing [Company]. We solve [problem] with AI-powered [feature]. Join the future of [category]." Announces the company without delivering resolution. Vague aspiration instead of specific vision.

### 4. Writing Quality & Flow

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does every line earn the next?** Is there a logical cause-and-effect chain throughout, or are there places where the logic breaks and the reader has to do the work of connecting sentences?
- **Can any sentence be reordered without losing meaning?** If yes, the logical chain is broken at that point. Each sentence should depend on the one before it and set up the one after it.
- **Is there any vague language where concrete language was available?** "Significant impact," "meaningful results," "transformative outcomes" -- these phrases are placeholders for claims the writer didn't make specific. Every instance is a missed opportunity.
- **Are transitions logical -- does "as a result" actually follow from a cause? Does "but" signal a real contradiction?** Connective language that doesn't connect is worse than no connector at all -- it signals structure while delivering none.
- **Does the writing have rhythm and momentum -- does it pull the reader forward, or does it require effort to continue?** Momentum comes from sentence variety, precise word choice, and logical escalation. Monotony kills momentum.
- **Are there any lines that break the reader's immersion -- that feel like vendor language appearing in what should be buyer language?** "Our proprietary technology" in the middle of a buyer-empathy section breaks immersion. The narrative should stay in the buyer's world until the pivot.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Every line earns the next. No vague language. Logical cause-and-effect throughout. Rhythm pulls the reader forward. No immersion-breaking vendor language. |
| **4/5** | Strong flow with one or two transitions that require a small logical leap. Minor vague language. Rhythm mostly holds. |
| **3/5** | Several transitions that break the logical chain. Mix of concrete and vague language. Rhythm inconsistent -- some sections pull, others require effort. |
| **2/5** | Majority of transitions are logical leaps rather than earned connections. Heavy vague language. Reads as a list of true statements rather than a flowing argument. |
| **1/5** | No logical flow. Sentences could be reordered freely. Vague throughout. No momentum. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** Each sentence is the logical consequence of the one before it. The reader never has to ask "why does that follow?" The narrative has the momentum of a well-constructed argument -- every paragraph ends with a tension that the next paragraph resolves, creating a pull that carries the reader to the end.
- **Weak (1/2):** True statements arranged in proximity but not in logical sequence. The reader understands each sentence individually but the connection between them requires interpretive work. Reads like bullet points converted to paragraphs.

### 5. Overall Narrative Strength

**What to evaluate:**

- **Does the named hero problem feel memorable and ownable by the end?** Could someone repeat it in a meeting after reading the POV once? If the reader can't recall the name, the narrative hasn't done its job.
- **Does the whole POV hold together as a single argument?** Or does it feel like multiple arguments loosely connected? A strong POV has the feeling of inevitability -- every section contributes to one thesis.
- **Does the company introduction feel earned -- the inevitable answer -- or does it feel like a gear shift?** The transition from diagnosis to solution is the moment the entire narrative either succeeds or fails. If the reader feels the shift, the arc wasn't tight enough.
- **Would the target buyer finish reading this and feel urgency -- a genuine sense that they need to act -- or would they feel informed but unmoved?** Information doesn't drive action. Felt urgency does. The POV must create emotional stakes, not just intellectual ones.
- **Is the POV differentiated from how every other company in the category talks?** Or does it sound like an industry white paper? The strongest test: could a competitor publish this POV with their name on it? If yes, it's not a point of view -- it's a category overview.

**Scoring guide:**

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5/5** | Hero problem is memorable and ownable. Single cohesive argument throughout. Company introduction feels inevitable. Buyer finishes reading with urgency. Differentiated from category noise. |
| **4/5** | Strong overall but hero problem name is slightly forgettable or one section feels slightly disconnected from the whole. |
| **3/5** | POV holds together structurally but doesn't create urgency. Reads as well-written but not compelling. Hero problem is named but not memorable. |
| **2/5** | Multiple disconnected arguments. Company introduction feels like a gear shift. Reader finishes informed but unmoved. |
| **1/5** | No coherent argument. Reads like a collection of marketing claims. Indistinguishable from category noise. |

**Calibration examples:**

- **Strong (5/5):** The reader finishes the POV and can name the hero problem from memory. They feel urgency -- not because they were told the problem is urgent, but because the narrative made them feel it. The company's introduction was the only thing that could have come next. No competitor could publish this POV.
- **Weak (2/5):** The reader finishes and thinks "that was well-written" but can't recall the specific hero problem name. The company introduction felt like a tonal shift. The POV could have been published by any of three competitors in the category.

## What a Perfect POV Looks Like

A 5/5 across all dimensions means:

1. **The first sentence drops the reader into their specific reality with no throat-clearing.** The buyer feels seen before they've finished the first paragraph.
2. **All seven beats are present in sequence, each earning the next, with failed alternatives explicitly named before the pivot.** No beats are skipped. No beats are out of order. Failed Tries are the proof that makes the pivot feel inevitable.
3. **The back half delivers mechanistic resolution, a specific "other side," and a world-changing closing vision.** The resolution is proportional to the problem. The closing vision extends beyond the product to a new world order.
4. **Every line earns the next through logical cause-and-effect -- no vague language, no broken transitions, no immersion-breaking vendor speak.** The narrative has the momentum of a well-constructed argument from first sentence to last.
5. **The reader finishes with urgency, a memorable hero problem name, and the feeling that the company's introduction was inevitable.** The POV is differentiated from category noise. No competitor could publish it.

**The total score range is 5-25.** Use this guide:

| Score | Assessment |
|-------|------------|
| **22-25** | Production-ready. POV can anchor homepage, pitch deck, and sales narrative without revision. |
| **18-21** | Strong foundation. One or two dimensions need tightening before the POV drives downstream deliverables. |
| **13-17** | Structural gaps. Arc is incomplete or resolution doesn't deliver on the tension built. Needs a revision pass before use. |
| **8-12** | Needs rework. Multiple dimensions weak. Using this POV as-is will produce generic, unconvincing downstream content. |
| **5-7** | Start over. The POV describes the category, not the company's specific point of view. |

## Common POV Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Throat-Clearing Opening

Starting with market size, company history, or industry trends before earning the right to the reader's attention. The first sentence must earn the second. "The global [category] market is projected to reach $X billion by 2030" is not an opening -- it's a stall. The reader doesn't care about the market. They care about their problem.

### Mistake 2: Skipping Failed Tries

The most commonly omitted beat. Without naming what buyers have already tried and why it failed, the pivot to the company feels like a sales pitch. Failed Tries are what make the company introduction feel inevitable rather than opportunistic. When a reader thinks "but what about [existing approach]?" and the POV hasn't addressed it, trust collapses.

### Mistake 3: Hero Problem Named Too Early

Naming the hero problem in the first paragraph before tension has been built. The hero problem should feel like the inevitable conclusion of everything that came before it -- not a thesis statement announced upfront. Academic papers lead with the thesis. Persuasive narratives earn the thesis. A POV is not a paper.

### Mistake 4: Feature-Based Solution Section

The back half introduces the product by listing features rather than explaining the mechanism. "We use AI to automate X and streamline Y" describes what the product does. "Every interaction deepens a living understanding of your business so advice becomes more precise over time" explains how the product solves the structural problem named in Beat 5. Features invite comparison. Mechanisms build belief.

### Mistake 5: Vague "Other Side"

"Imagine a world where legal is no longer a bottleneck" is aspirational but not specific. The "other side" should describe a concrete reality the buyer can picture: what their day looks like, what decisions they can now make, what they no longer have to tolerate. If the reader can't see the scene, they can't want it.

### Mistake 6: Closing Vision Is Too Small

Ending with a product outcome rather than a world-changing vision. The strongest POVs close by describing how the entire category or industry operates differently because this company exists -- not just how the buyer's team operates differently. "Your legal team moves faster" is a product outcome. "Elite legal judgment becomes as accessible as a Google search" is a world-changing vision. The difference is ambition.

### Mistake 7: Broken Logical Chain

Transitions that use connective language ("as a result," "therefore," "but") without the logical connection those words imply. Every transition must be earned -- the sentence after "as a result" must actually follow from what came before it. Unearned transitions are worse than no transitions at all because they promise coherence and deliver confusion.
